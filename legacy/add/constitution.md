# AD&D Constitution

This document is the creative doctrine of **Advanced Drummers and Dungeons**.

It exists to protect the project from drift, timidity, and boredom.
When plans, features, or implementations conflict with this document, this document wins.

## 1. What We Are Trying To Make

We are not trying to make a disposable idle game.
We are trying to make a game a player can become obsessed with for years.

That obsession must come from two places at once:

- the pleasure of **compulsive optimization**,
- and the pull of **emotional attachment** to a world that feels alive, strange, and worth returning to.

AD&D should feel like a place that keeps breathing while the player is away.
Coming back should never feel like reopening a spreadsheet.
It should feel like re-entering a living system with pressure, memory, consequence, and possibility.

## 2. Our Core Belief

**Sound is survival.**

This is not flavor.
This is not metaphor pasted on top of generic mechanics.
This is the foundation of the game.

Safety comes from sound.
Territory comes from sound.
Hope comes from sound.
Risk begins where sound fails.

If a mechanic weakens that truth, it weakens the project.

## 3. The Fantasy

The player fantasy is:

**I maintain a fragile refuge through sound, push beyond its protection into a hostile world, and build the means to go farther than I should.**

The player should feel:

- relief when returning to safety,
- hunger to optimize what keeps the refuge alive,
- curiosity about what lies beyond the edge,
- attachment to the systems, people, and places that make survival possible,
- and satisfaction when better planning transforms danger into reach.

## 4. The Ambition

AD&D must aim higher than competence.

It should be:

- richer than a standard idle game,
- deeper than a progression treadmill,
- stranger than familiar post-apocalyptic routine,
- and more authored than systems that hide emptiness behind scale.

We are explicitly pursuing:

- **complexity** that creates meaningful decisions,
- **density** that rewards attention,
- **mystery** that sustains curiosity,
- and **layered progression** that keeps opening new forms of mastery.

But depth is only valuable if it remains fun.
The point is not to impress ourselves with intricacy.
The point is to create long-term delight, fixation, and return.

## 5. Our Enemy

The enemy of this project is **boringness**.

Boringness includes:

- repetitive interaction without meaningful choice,
- systems with many parts but no emotional charge,
- lore with no effect on play,
- optimization without personality,
- tone without wit,
- scope without depth,
- and ambition that collapses into admin.

We do not fear being unusual.
We should fear being forgettable.

## 6. Fun Comes First

AD&D may be bleak, unhealthy, grotesque, tragic, or absurd.
It may be darkly funny.
It may be full of decay and pressure.

But it must remain fun.

If atmosphere and entertainment ever conflict, we do not choose flat seriousness over player pleasure.
We choose the version that keeps the game alive in the player's mind.

Fun in AD&D does not mean lightness.
It means momentum, surprise, appetite, payoff, attachment, and the pleasure of mastering something with character.

## 7. Tone

The tone of AD&D should be **darkly funny, rich, and alive**.

It should allow for:

- dread without self-importance,
- absurdity without parody,
- tenderness without sentimentality,
- ugliness without monotony,
- and humor sharp enough to make the world feel inhabited rather than decorative.

The world should feel written by people who enjoy it, not embalmed by people who only want it to sound serious.

## 8. The Living World Standard

The idle dimension of AD&D must support the feeling that the world continues while the player is away.

Offline time is not an accounting trick.
It is part of the fantasy.

Progression, return states, world pressures, and recoveries should strengthen the idea that:

- systems continue,
- costs continue,
- opportunities continue,
- and absence has texture.

The player should be able to leave, return, and immediately feel that the world has been in motion.

## 9. The Team's Creed

We should make decisions that increase:

- obsession,
- fun,
- ambition,
- legibility,
- emotional attachment,
- and replayable depth.

We should resist decisions that increase:

- noise,
- filler,
- false complexity,
- genericness,
- content sprawl without payoff,
- or caution masquerading as polish.

Our standard is not "good enough to function."
Our standard is "strong enough to keep pulling someone back."

## 10. What Must Not Be Diluted

As the project grows, we must protect:

- **fun**, so that depth remains pleasurable rather than dutiful,
- **ambition**, so that practicality does not turn the game ordinary,
- **sound as survival**, so the game keeps its identity,
- **richness of lore and design**, so optimization has texture and meaning,
- and **distinctiveness**, so the game cannot be mistaken for anything cheaper in spirit.

## 11. What We Refuse To Become

We refuse to become:

- a boring idle game with decorative lore,
- a design archive mistaken for a game,
- a toolbox that never converges into an experience,
- a project that confuses complication with depth,
- or a respectable but forgettable product.

## 12. Working Rule

When uncertain, choose the path that makes AD&D more:

- fun,
- obsessive,
- ambitious,
- rich,
- alive,
- and unmistakably itself.

That is the standard.
