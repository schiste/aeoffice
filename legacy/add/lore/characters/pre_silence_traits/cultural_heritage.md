# Cultural Heritage

> **Category:** Background & Origin
>
> **Template Field:** `Cultural Heritage`
>
> **Dialog Impact:** Traditions, food references, holidays, family expectations

---

## Overview

Cultural heritage provides the unspoken assumptions, references, and values that shape how characters see the world. This affects everything from holiday stress to food preferences to concepts of family obligation.

---

## Elements to Define

### Primary Cultural Background

Consider:
- **Ethnicity/ancestry:** Where did their family originate?
- **Immigration generation:** First-gen, second-gen, further removed?
- **Cultural identification:** Do they strongly identify? Assimilated? Complicated?
- **Religious background:** Practicing, cultural, lapsed, none?

### How It Manifests in Dialog

| Element | Example Manifestations |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Food references** | Comfort foods, cooking traditions, "my grandmother's [dish]" |
| **Holiday stress** | Which holidays matter, family obligations, travel |
| **Family expectations** | Career pressure, marriage, children, caring for elders |
| **Proverbs/sayings** | Native language phrases, cultural wisdom |
| **Naming conventions** | Full names, nicknames, titles, formality |
| **Taboo topics** | What's not discussed, shame, honor |

## Sample Cultural Profiles

### East Asian-American (Chinese, Korean, Japanese, Vietnamese, etc.)

**Common Dialog Markers:**
- Education and achievement pressure
- Family hierarchy and respect
- "Face" and public reputation concerns
- Food as love language
- Intergenerational household considerations

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"My parents didn't come all this way for me to fail."
"We eat together. That's non-negotiable."
"I can't complain to my mother—she'd never let it go."
"Face it—this reflects on the whole family."
```

**Sleepless Context:**
- May feel shame about "complaining"
- Family pressure on child-rearing
- Multigenerational advice/interference

---

### South Asian-American (Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, etc.)

**Common Dialog Markers:**
- Extended family involvement
- Professional expectations (doctor, engineer, lawyer)
- Marriage and family pressure
- Hospitality expectations
- Community reputation

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"Every auntie has an opinion. Every single one."
"I'm the first in my family to not become a doctor. So."
"You don't understand—it's not just us. It's the whole community."
"My mother would have a heart attack if she knew."
```

**Sleepless Context:**
- May not want family knowing about problems
- Community gossip concerns
- Different approach to authority/complaints

---

### Latino/Hispanic-American

**Common Dialog Markers:**
- Familismo—family comes first
- Warmth and expressiveness
- Catholic/religious references (if applicable)
- Code-switching between English and Spanish
- Respeto—respect for elders

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"Familia is everything. That's why this is so hard."
"My abuela would know what to do."
"We're loud. That's how we are. But this is different."
"I'll light a candle. At this point, why not?"
```

**Sleepless Context:**
- May have extended family input
- Different noise tolerance baseline
- Communal problem-solving approach

---

### African-American

**Common Dialog Markers:**
- Historical awareness and context
- Community and church connections
- Code-switching (professional vs. home)
- Resilience framing
- Distrust of institutions (earned)

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"We've dealt with worse. But my kids shouldn't have to."
"I'm not calling the cops. That's not an option."
"The neighborhood is changing. We're being pushed out."
"My grandmother raised five kids in worse conditions. But still."
```

**Sleepless Context:**
- May distrust official channels
- Community solutions preferred
- Gentrification concerns
- Different relationship with authority

---

### European-American (White American)

**Notes:** This category is diverse—Irish, Italian, German, Jewish, WASP, etc. Each has distinct markers.

**WASP (White Anglo-Saxon Protestant)**
```
"We don't talk about these things publicly."
"There's a proper way to handle this."
"I'm not comfortable with... confrontation."
```

**Jewish-American**
```
"This isn't our first persecution, but it is annoying."
"My mother's guilt could power a city."
"Nu? So what are we going to do about it?"
```

**Italian-American**
```
"Family dinners are loud. We're loud. This is different."
"Sunday dinner is sacred."
"My nonna would just go yell at them. I miss that option."
```

**Irish-American**
```
"We handle problems with humor. Dark humor."
"My family's been complaining for generations. It's genetic."
"Pour me another. I'll deal with it tomorrow."
```

---

### Middle Eastern/North African

**Common Dialog Markers:**
- Hospitality expectations
- Family honor concepts
- Religious observance (if applicable)
- Post-9/11 awareness and caution
- Warm expressiveness

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"We opened our home to neighbors. This is how we're repaid?"
"Privacy is important. Family matters stay in the family."
"I'm careful what I complain about. And to whom."
"Inshallah, it will resolve itself. But probably not."
```

## Cultural Complexity

### Mixed Heritage
- "I'm half Korean, half Irish. I got the guilt from both sides."
- "My parents had very different ideas about... everything."

### Assimilation Tensions
- "My parents want me to be 'American.' But also not forget where we came from."
- "I don't speak the language. I feel like a fraud at family gatherings."

### Cultural Reclamation
- "I'm reconnecting with traditions my parents let go."
- "My generation is doing things differently."

---

## Writing Notes

- **Avoid stereotypes:** These are starting points, not definitions
- **Individual variation:** Not everyone from a culture fits patterns
- **Ask why:** Why does this character express or suppress heritage?
- **Intersectionality:** Heritage combines with class, education, region
