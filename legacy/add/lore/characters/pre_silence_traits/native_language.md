# Native Language

> **Category:** Language & Communication
>
> **Template Field:** `Native Language`
>
> **Dialog Impact:** Emotional expression, idioms, thought patterns

---

## Overview

Native language shapes how characters think, what idioms they reach for, and how they express emotion. Even for multilingual characters, the native language is where they return under stress.

---

## Common Languages

### English (American)

**Characteristics:**
- Direct communication style
- Pop culture references
- Regional variations (Southern, New York, Midwest, etc.)
- Informal default in many contexts

**Dialog Markers:**
- Casual contractions
- Sports and business metaphors
- Pop culture references
- "You know what I mean?"

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"Let's cut to the chase."
"That's a home run / touchdown / slam dunk."
"This is like that episode of..."
"I mean, come on."
```

**Under Stress:**
- Swearing tends toward simplicity
- Directness increases
- May become more regional

---

### English (British)

**Characteristics:**
- Understatement as art form
- Class markers in vocabulary
- Different idioms and references
- Formal/informal distinctions

**Dialog Markers:**
- "Rather," "quite," "proper"
- Understatement ("not ideal" = terrible)
- Tea and weather references
- Apologizing reflexively

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"That's... not ideal."
"I'm not being funny, but..."
"Shall we?"
"Sorry, but..."
```

**Under Stress:**
- Becomes more clipped
- Understatement increases
- May become more formal

---

### French

**Characteristics:**
- Formal/informal (tu/vous) consciousness
- Expressive emotional vocabulary
- Untranslatable concepts
- Cultural pride in language

**Dialog Markers:**
- May code-switch for precision
- "C'est..." expressions
- French phrases for emotions
- Formal register awareness

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"It's not quite... comment dire... that."
"There's no word in English. C'est du n'importe quoi."
"Bon, okay, let's try again."
"You understand? Non? Tant pis."
```

**Under Stress:**
- Reverts to French cursing
- French expressions increase
- May struggle for English words

**Relevant to Setting:**
- Founder Marc Delacroix is French
- "Founder's French" becomes a dialect marker post-Silence

---

### German

**Characteristics:**
- Precision and compound words
- Direct communication valued
- Formal structures
- Efficiency-focused

**Dialog Markers:**
- Precise word choice
- May create compound concepts
- Directness without softening
- "This is how it is"

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"Let me be precise about this."
"There's a German word: Schadenfreude. Also Weltschmerz."
"That is incorrect."
"We have a saying: [German proverb]."
```

**Under Stress:**
- Becomes more clipped, precise
- German words emerge
- May seem harsh to English speakers

---

### Spanish

**Characteristics:**
- Warmth and expressiveness
- Formal/informal (usted/tú) consciousness
- Family and diminutive language
- Regional variations (Mexican, Argentine, Spanish, etc.)

**Dialog Markers:**
- Diminutives for affection
- Family references
- Expressive emotional range
- Code-switching natural

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"Ay, mijo, this is too much."
"No es posible. How can they do this?"
"Mi amor, I'm so tired."
"Pues, what can you do?"
```

**Under Stress:**
- Spanish expressions increase
- Emotional vocabulary
- May pray in Spanish

---

### Mandarin / Cantonese

**Characteristics:**
- Tonal awareness
- Family vs. professional language
- Respect and hierarchy markers
- Cultural concepts without translation

**Dialog Markers:**
- May search for English equivalents
- Family terms specific
- Harmony and face concepts
- Different emotional expression norms

**Sample Phrases:**
```
"In Chinese we say... but there's no translation."
"My parents would say this is about face."
"The harmony is broken. That's the problem."
"You don't understand the family pressure."
```

**Under Stress:**
- May become quieter (cultural norm)
- Family language emerges
- Concepts resist translation

### Other Languages

For characters with other native languages, consider:

| Language | Key Characteristics | Stress Response |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Arabic** | Formal registers, religious phrases, hospitality language | Religious invocations |
| **Hindi/Urdu** | Formal/informal distinctions, family terms, English mixing | Hindi expressions |
| **Japanese** | Politeness levels, indirect communication, context-heavy | Becomes more formal or silent |
| **Korean** | Age-based speech, formal hierarchy, English mixing | Korean expressions |
| **Portuguese** | Warmth, saudade concept, Brazilian vs. European | Portuguese exclamations |
| **Russian** | Direct, grammatically complex thoughts, dark humor | Russian cursing |
| **Italian** | Expressive, gesture-integrated, regional pride | Italian expressions |
| **Dutch** | Direct, efficient, comfortable with English | Dutch directness increases |

## Language and Emotion

Native language often governs emotional expression:

| Situation | Native Language Effect |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Swearing | Reverts to native curses |
| Affection | Native endearments |
| Prayer/stress | Native appeals |
| Dreams | Often in native language |
| Counting | Native numbers |
| Extreme fatigue | Native language dominates |

---

## Language and Sleepless Context

Native language affects group dynamics:
- Shared native language creates instant bond
- Different languages can isolate or connect
- Translation of key concepts matters
- Multilingual members bridge gaps
