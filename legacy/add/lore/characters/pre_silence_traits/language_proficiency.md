# Language Proficiency

> **Category:** Language & Communication
>
> **Template Field:** `Second Language(s)`
>
> **Dialog Impact:** Code-switching, accent bleed, translation ability

---

## Overview

Second language proficiency determines when and how characters code-switch, what they can or can't express, and how they navigate multilingual environments.

---

## Proficiency Levels

### Native

**Definition:** Learned from birth or early childhood. Full fluency.

**Characteristics:**
- Thinks in this language
- Full emotional expression
- Native accent
- Idioms natural
- Cultural fluency included

**Dialog Markers:**
```
[No markers—sounds like native speaker]
May use as "secret" language with other native speakers
Full range of registers available
```

---

### Fluent

**Definition:** Near-native command. May have learned as older child or immersed adult.

**Characteristics:**
- Rare errors
- Occasional odd phrasing
- Slight accent possible
- Full comprehension
- Can joke, argue, express nuance

**Dialog Markers:**
```
"What's the word... oh, [correct word]."
Very occasional articles/preposition errors
May pause for idioms
"In my other language, we'd say..."
```

---

### Professional

**Definition:** Full work competence. Can handle meetings, documents, negotiations.

**Characteristics:**
- Strong formal vocabulary
- Weaker in casual registers
- Noticeable accent
- Can discuss complex topics
- Emotional expression limited

**Dialog Markers:**
```
"I can do meetings in [language], but jokes are hard."
Formal vocabulary strong, slang weak
May misread tone
Accent noticeable
"How do you say [concept] casually?"
```

---

### Conversational

**Definition:** Can discuss most topics with native speakers. Noticeable gaps.

**Characteristics:**
- Handles daily situations
- Struggles with specialized topics
- Obvious accent
- May ask for clarification
- Needs patience from native speakers

**Dialog Markers:**
```
"Sorry, can you say that again?"
Simplified sentence structures
Pauses to find words
May mix in native language
"I understand more than I can say."
```

---

### Basic

**Definition:** Simple exchanges. Tourist-level. Limited vocabulary.

**Characteristics:**
- Greetings, directions, numbers
- Cannot have real conversation
- May understand more than they speak
- Relies on gestures, help
- Takes effort to communicate

**Dialog Markers:**
```
"I know a little [language]. Very little."
Single words and phrases
May resort to English/native quickly
"How do you say [basic thing]?"
Visible effort and frustration
```

---

### Passive / Heritage

**Definition:** Understands but doesn't speak well. Often heritage language from family.

**Characteristics:**
- Grandparent/parent spoke it
- Comprehension much stronger than production
- Emotional connection to language
- May feel shame about not speaking
- Can follow conversations

**Dialog Markers:**
```
"My grandmother spoke [language]. I understood her but couldn't reply."
"I can follow it. Don't ask me to say anything."
"I feel like a fraud at family gatherings."
"I've been meaning to learn it properly."
May understand jokes others miss
```

## Code-Switching Patterns

How multilingual characters switch:

| Trigger | Switch Pattern |
|---------|---------------|
| Emotion | To native/most comfortable language |
| Precision | To language with best word |
| Audience | Match the room |
| Secrecy | Language others don't know |
| Comfort | Mix languages freely |

**Sample Code-Switching:**
```
"It's just—ay, no sé—it's like they don't even see us."
"The problem is Gemütlichkeit. Coziness but more. English doesn't have it."
"(in French to spouse) Should we tell them?"
"She was being very... how do you say... passive-aggressive?"
```

## Accent Bleed

Second language can affect native speech:
- Rhythm patterns
- Pronunciation of certain sounds
- Idiom translation (calques)
- False friends

**Examples:**
```
"I must go now" (instead of "I have to go")
Rolling R sounds
Different stress patterns
"It makes me nothing" (translated idiom)
```

## Language Learning Context

How they learned affects how they speak:

| Learning Context | Result |
|-----------------|--------|
| School/formal | Good grammar, weak slang |
| Immersion | Natural but gaps |
| Partner/family | Good casual, weak formal |
| Work | Technical vocabulary strong |
| Self-taught | Unpredictable |
