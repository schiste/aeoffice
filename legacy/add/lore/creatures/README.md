## Why Animals Survived (And Humans Didn't)

### The Scientific Truth

When Marie Fontaine designed Hush, the Sounding Five spent **three months** debating the ethics of potentially harming animals. The debate was passionate, emotional, and surprisingly lengthy.

Their conclusion: **Hush must be calibrated to spare animals.**

Marie adjusted the viral payload to target the specific neural architecture of human auditory processing — particularly the **planum temporale** and **Wernicke's area**, regions uniquely developed in humans for language comprehension and complex sound-meaning association.

Animals hear. Humans *listen*. Marie designed Hush to destroy the listening.

**The Calibration:**
- Human auditory cortex: ~100 billion neural connections for sound processing
- Animal auditory systems: 1-10 billion connections (varies by species)
- Hush threshold: Set to trigger cascading neural failure at >50 billion connections
- Result: Humans die. Animals experience... something else.

### The Unintended Consequence

Marie was brilliant. Marie was also wrong.

Hush didn't kill animals. But the sudden, traumatic disruption of their auditory neural pathways triggered **accelerated evolutionary pressure**. The virus didn't destroy their hearing — it *destabilized* it.

Animals that survived the initial shock found their sensory processing... reorganizing. Neural plasticity went into overdrive. Generations that should have taken millennia compressed into decades.

The Sounding Five wanted to protect animals from suffering.

They created something much worse.

## The Irony

The most passionate advocate for animal protection in the Sounding Five was **Jonathan Reeves** (@CribRocker), who spent his considerable wealth on wildlife conservation before The Silence.

His estate in the Loire Valley is now a **mutation nest**.

The animals he protected became the creatures that hunt his descendants.

## The Last Human(s)

*See [the_last_humans.md](the_last_humans.md) for the complete list.*

When immune survivors existed but couldn't survive the new world, each death became legend. Every region has their "Last Human" — and they're all equally "the last."

**Known "Last Humans":**
- Jean-Michel (Year 12 AS) — Brittany
- The Hermit of Lyon (Year 23 AS) — claimed to be "the actual last"
- Old Marie (Year 31 AS) — reportedly the "real" last human
- The Three Brothers (Year 38 AS) — all three claimed to be "the last"
- Bernard (Year 41 AS) — left a note saying "I am the last human, don't let anyone tell you otherwise"
- Simone (Year 42 AS) — current scholarly consensus "last human" (disputed)

The debate about who was *really* the last human continues in academic circles.

## Creating New Creatures

Use **[CREATURE_TEMPLATE.md](CREATURE_TEMPLATE.md)** for all new creature entries. The template includes:

- **Quick Reference Card** — "Pokémon-style" stats at a glance
- **Biological Profile** — Size, weight, integument, thermoregulation, locomotion
- **Sensory Systems** — The critical "Sound Question" (every creature's relationship to the acoustic apocalypse)
- **Ecology & Behavior** — Diet, habitat, reproduction, social structure
- **Threat Assessment** — Danger ratings, encounter scenarios, combat protocols
- **Origin & Evolution** — Connection to pre-Silence ancestor and Hush effects
- **Cultural Significance** — Idioms, folklore, utility, and The Irony
- **Research History** — Discovery, notable incidents, open questions

The template ensures consistency while leaving room for the darkly absurd humor that makes our creatures memorable.

## Tone Guidance

When writing creatures:

1. **Derangingly familiar** — Start with recognition, end with horror
2. **Darkly absurd** — The rabbit has poisonous claws AND it's adorable
3. **Ecological logic** — Every mutation has a reason (even if ridiculous)
4. **Sound-centric** — Everything relates back to the acoustic apocalypse
5. **French flavor** — Use folklore, regional references, Loire Valley specifics

**Example tone:**

> The Lapin Rouge looks like a rabbit. It hops like a rabbit. It even makes a sound that, if you squint with your ears, might be mistaken for a rabbit.
>
> It is not a rabbit.
>
> The claws were the first adaptation — necessary after they lost the ability to hear predators. The venom came second. The size came third.
>
> A full-grown Lapin Rouge stands about knee-height on a human. They travel in groups of 12-20, called a "murder" (the old term was "fluffle," but that felt inappropriate after Year 3 AS).
>
> They're still technically herbivores. They just became very aggressive about it.
