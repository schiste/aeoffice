# Geography of Year 300

> **Overview:** The physical world of Year 300 — where sound means death and silence means survival. This section covers settlements, dangerous zones, trade routes, and the transformed landscape of post-Silence Europe.

---

## The Core Principle

**Sound attracts. Silence survives.**

The geography of Year 300 is shaped entirely by this reality. Settlements exist where:
1. Natural sound barriers provide protection (caves, valleys, dense forest)
2. Distance from creature territories allows some noise
3. Resources justify the risk of habitation

---

## The Known World

### Primary Setting: France

The game is centered in what was once France, now simply called **"the land"** or **"the middle territories"** by most inhabitants. Year 300 people have limited geographic knowledge beyond their immediate regions.

**Why France?**
- Central European location (trade crossroads)
- Diverse geography (caves, mountains, coasts, forests)
- The Bresme Caves as anchor location
- Sufficient distance from the worst creature concentrations

### Geographic Zones

| Zone | Characteristics | Danger Level | Notable Settlements |
|------|-----------------|--------------|---------------------|
| **The Deep Caves** | Underground networks, natural silence | Low-Medium | Bresme, The Archive |
| **The Quiet Forests** | Dense canopy, sound absorption | Medium | Scattered woodfolk |
| **The Open Fields** | Former farmland, exposed | High | Abandoned, transient camps only |
| **The Dead Cities** | Urban ruins, echo hazards | Extreme | None permanent |
| **The Coasts** | Wave noise provides cover | Medium-High | Fisher settlements |
| **The Mountains** | Echo danger, but defensible | High | Isolated hermitages |

## Key Concepts

### Sound Zones

The world is mentally mapped by **sound safety**, not traditional geography:

**Safe Zones (Green):**
- Deep caves with sound-absorbing stone
- Dense old-growth forests
- Underground rivers (white noise cover)
- Certain coastal areas (wave cover)

**Caution Zones (Yellow):**
- Forest edges
- River crossings
- Ruins with good escape routes
- Known creature patrol gaps

**Death Zones (Red):**
- Open plains and fields
- City centers (echo amplification)
- Mountain passes (sound funneling)
- Known creature territories

### The Creature Factor

Geography is inseparable from creature distribution. See [Creatures folder](../creatures/) for details.

**General principle:** Creatures are more concentrated in:
- Former population centers (cities had more... material)
- Areas with good acoustics for hunting
- Regions with underground access points

### Seasonal Geography

The map changes with seasons:

**Summer:**
- Longer travel windows (more daylight for silent movement)
- Creatures more active (warmer = more hunting)
- Forest travel easier (dry ground, less noise)

**Winter:**
- Snow muffles sound (advantage)
- Snow crunches underfoot (danger)
- Creatures less active but more desperate
- Travel restricted to known paths

**The Storms:**
- Heavy rain provides sound cover for movement
- Thunder allows emergency loud activities
- Storm-running is a specialized survival skill

## Geographic Knowledge in Year 300

### What People Know

**Local knowledge (high):**
- Their immediate territory (5-10 km radius)
- Nearby settlements and relationships
- Local creature patterns
- Safe and dangerous micro-zones

**Regional knowledge (medium):**
- Major settlements within trading distance
- Primary trade routes
- General danger zones
- Seasonal patterns

**Distant knowledge (low/mythological):**
- Other countries exist (maybe)
- The sea continues beyond the horizon
- Cities were once everywhere
- The world was once loud and people survived

### Maps

Year 300 maps are:
- Hand-drawn on leather, cloth, or waraq
- Focused on sound zones rather than political boundaries
- Constantly updated as creature patterns shift
- Closely guarded (a good map is worth lives)

**The Bresme Map Room** contains the most comprehensive regional mapping, maintained by generations of scouts.

## Design Notes (Internal)

### For Writers
- Geography should always connect to sound/silence mechanics
- Every location has a "sound profile" — how safe is normal conversation?
- Travel is never trivial; distance = danger
- Weather matters (sound cover opportunities)

### For Game Masters
- Use geography to create tension (safe zone → danger zone transitions)
- Seasonal changes affect encounter probability
- Maps can be quest rewards
- "Geographic knowledge" is a valuable character trait

### Connections to Other Lore
- The Bresme Cave Code (Article 6) references cave acoustics
- The Lindquist family has surface travel expertise
- Trade goods from worldbuilding entries move along these routes
- Creature territories explain why artifacts are where they are
