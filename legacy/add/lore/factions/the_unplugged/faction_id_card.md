## Identity & Beliefs

### Core Identity
> **"We are the prepared."**

The Unplugged define themselves by foresight, planning, and adherence to tradition. Their ancestors survived because they prepared. Survival depends on following the rules that kept them alive.

### What They Believe Caused The Silence
**Terrorist attack** — origin unknown, perpetrators unknown. They do not speculate or blame without evidence. The silence came; they survived; that is what matters.

### Core Values

| Value | Expression |

| **Tradition** | Rules exist because they work. Follow them. |
| **Preparation** | Plan for everything. Chaos kills. |

### The Council

Simply "The Council" — there is only one. They are survivors in caves; they don't need a fancier name.

- One representative per family (~400 members in Year 300)
- Meets to decide community matters
- Elects the Dadi every 12 years
- Each family chooses its own representative (no universal rule)

### Le Dadi (The Leader)

**Title:** Dadi

**Origin:** A lost joke. Before The Silence, Henri Marchand would jokingly tell people on the Telegram group "Call me Daddy." Nobody liked it much, but it became a running joke. When Henri led the survivors into the caves, people started calling him "Daddy" partly in jest, partly in respect. After his death (Year 1 AS, Month 3), the title was formalized as "Dadi" — the pronunciation drifted, the spelling changed, and the joke was forgotten.

By Year 300, "Dadi" is a serious, respected title. Scholars debate its etymology. Some think it means "the one who provides." Others connect it to old French words. No one remembers a sleep-deprived dad making a bad joke on Telegram.

**The irony is perfect.**

### The Current Dadi (Year 300 AS)

**Name:** Sylvain Marchand
**Family:** Marchand
**Term:** Year 296 - Year 308 AS
**Age:** 54

Sylvain is the first Marchand to hold the Dadi title since the Great Marchand-Delacroix Feud of Year 210 AS. For 90 years, no Marchand dared run for Dadi — the political cost was too high. The Delacroix had made it clear: vote for a Marchand, and you vote against the legacy of Marc himself.

**The Year 210 Feud:**
The conflict began over a proposed cave expansion that would have relocated several Delacroix family quarters. The Marchands supported it; the Delacroix opposed. What should have been a routine Council vote escalated into accusations, counter-accusations, and eventually a public shouting match between the family heads. The sitting Dadi (a Delacroix) used procedural maneuvers to kill the proposal. The Marchands withdrew from active politics in protest.

For 86 years, the Marchands remained neutral — influential behind the scenes, but never seeking the Dadi seat.

**Why Sylvain won:**
- The expansion crisis forced change — the caves are full, and the Delacroix "stay the course" approach is failing
- Sylvain campaigned on pragmatism: "We need new territory, not old feuds"
- The Lindquist mission to Studio Echo was his idea — give the dangerous job to the family with no political ties
- Younger families, tired of the Marchand-Delacroix cold war, voted for change

**Sylvain's character:**
- Quiet, methodical, deeply pragmatic
- A Brassensiste (philosophy over emotion)
- Knows his family holds the encrypted drive; hasn't decided what to do with it
- Believes the community's survival matters more than old secrets
- Genuinely surprised the Hero succeeded at Studio Echo — now dealing with consequences he didn't fully anticipate

**Political position:**
Sylvain is officially neutral, but his actions favor the Diggers and Expansionists. The Earthbound distrust him. The Delacroix are waiting for him to fail.

### The Family System

Families are the core social unit:
- Each family elects one representative to the Council (method chosen by family)
- Families manage internal affairs (resource allocation, discipline, education)
- Marriage creates new family branches or merges existing ones
- Family size is tracked for demographic planning
- Families are responsible for ensuring population growth
- Each family handles succession however they want — eldest, vote, merit — as long as there is one recognized head who represents the family

**Everyone belongs to a family.** There are no orphans without placement, no exiles without lineage. Orphaned children are adopted into families. Marriages merge or create family branches. The family system is total — you cannot exist outside it.

This means:
- Social identity comes from family
- Resources are allocated through families
- Disputes are between families, mediated by the Council
- A family-less person would be inconceivable

- Young people have nowhere to go

This is the context for the Hero's mission to Studio Echo — not just curiosity, but necessity. The Unplugged need new territory. The Hero's discovery of a Crystal changes everything.

## Daily Life

### Cave Room Allocation

The cave system is divided into chambers, tunnels, and quarters. Families tend to live together — not by formal rule, but by centuries of habit.

**How it works:**
- Entire chambers or sections are associated with specific families
- The Delacroix Quarter, the Moreau Tunnels, the Vidal Farms — names have stuck for generations
- When couples form across families, they typically move to one family's section or claim new space (if available)
- Children usually stay in their birth-family's area until marriage
- Some families are relaxed about members living elsewhere; Traditionalist families (Duvals, Martinets) strongly prefer everyone stays close

**The crowding problem:**
With the caves at capacity, space disputes have intensified. Young couples struggle to find quarters. Some families have split into sub-branches occupying different sections. The expansion crisis isn't abstract — it's felt every day in cramped sleeping chambers and arguments over who gets the room vacated by a death.

### Daily Schedule

Life in the caves follows a structured rhythm designed for both survival and mental health.

**The Mandatory Arts:**
Every day includes designated time for art and entertainment. This isn't optional — it's understood that humans need more than survival to thrive. After 300 years, the schedule is deeply ingrained:

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| **Morning** | Work shifts begin (farming, maintenance, crafts, teaching) |
| **Midday** | Communal meal in family sections |
| **Afternoon** | Work continues; children in education |
| **Evening** | **Mandatory Arts Hour** — music, storytelling, games, dancing |
| **Night** | Family time, sleep |

**The Mandatory Arts Hour:**
- Families gather in common areas
- Vinyl is played (on strict rotation to preserve the collection)
- Stories are told — oral history, fiction, gossip
- Games are played — cards, dice, board games preserved from the Before
- Dancing happens (especially among Trenétistes)
- Instruments are played — drums, flutes, stringed instruments crafted over generations

Missing Mandatory Arts without reason is frowned upon. It's not punished formally, but neighbors notice. "He doesn't come to Arts" is a serious criticism — it suggests someone isn't contributing to collective wellbeing.

**Why it works:**
The Unplugged learned early that pure survival isn't enough. The first generation saw depression, conflict, and despair nearly destroy the community. The Mandatory Arts emerged from that crisis — a formal commitment to joy as essential as food.

## Education

### Philosophy

Education is paramount. The Unplugged believe — correctly — that knowledge is survival. After 300 years of refinement, the education system is remarkably efficient and, surprisingly, fun.

The Beaumont family runs the schools, but the curriculum is community-approved. Every child learns:
- Reading, writing, mathematics (the basics)
- Cave ecology (mushroom cultivation, water systems, ventilation)
- History (The Silence, the founders, the Before)
- Practical skills (every child learns at least one trade)
- Music (vinyl theology, instrument basics, the sacred songs)

### Selection Day

At age 8, children undergo **Assessment** — a comprehensive evaluation by adults from multiple families. They're tested on aptitude, interest, physical ability, and temperament. The results are "graded scientifically" (a phrase that means less than it sounds, but the Beaumonts take it very seriously).

Based on Assessment, each child receives a list of **Options** — roles they're suited for. A child might be offered: Farmer, Drummer, or Teacher. Another might get: Digger, Builder, or Guard.

Then comes **Selection Day**.

**How Selection Day Works:**

1. **Round 1 — Uncontested Choices:** Children announce their first choice. If there are 3 doctor positions and exactly 3 children want to be doctors, done. No conflict, no contest.

2. **Round 2 — Contested Choices:** If 5 children want 3 farmer positions, they compete. Contests are practical: two children want to be diggers? They dig. Two want to be musicians? They play. The better performer wins the spot.

3. **Round 3 and beyond:** Losers return to their remaining options. Process repeats until everyone is placed.

**The system's elegance:**
- Children get meaningful choice (not pure assignment)
- Merit matters (you earn your role)
- Losers aren't shamed — they simply move to their next option
- The community gets people suited to their roles

**The Perfect Year (Year 288 AS):**
For the first time in history, Selection Day completed in **one round**. Every single position was filled by exactly the right number of candidates. No contests needed. No second choices. Perfect alignment.

The Beaumonts called it "the miracle of matching." Statisticians calculate the odds at roughly 1 in 4 million. Some attribute it to careful Assessment. Others to luck. The Lindquists claim the lectromusic blessed the children that year (they were ignored).

Year 288 is now celebrated as **Harmony Year** in educational circles.

**What happens after Selection:**
Selected roles become apprenticeships. A child selected as "Digger" apprentices with the Lefebvre family. A "Healer" apprentices with the Rousseaus. By age 16 (Coming of Age), most children are skilled practitioners.

**Changing roles:**
Rare but possible. A failed digger can petition to retrain as a farmer. The Council must approve. The original family (Lefebvres, in this case) must consent. Most requests are granted — forcing someone into work they hate benefits no one.

## Justice System

> **"We don't have prisons. We have shame, patience, and very dusty floors."**

The Unplugged approach justice like they approach everything: with an absurd commitment to process, absolute faith in social pressure, and the unshakeable belief that being lectured by a Martinet is punishment enough for any crime.

### The Three Tiers

#### Tier 1: Family Resolution

Most disputes never leave the family cave. The Family Head mediates, usually over a shared meal.

**Common Resolutions:**
- The offender apologizes (sincerely)
- The offender performs extra family duties
- The offender listens to their aunt tell them exactly how disappointed she is (often sufficient)
- The matter is declared "resolved" and never spoken of again (it will be spoken of again)

**If Resolution Fails:** Matter escalates to The Watch.

#### Tier 2: The Watch

**The Martinets** serve as the community's historians, crime novel writers, and investigators. In a secluded underground community, this is as close as anyone gets to professional detectives — and they take it *extremely* seriously.

See [major_families.md](major_families.md) for full Martinet details.

**The Martinet Character:**
- Very solemn. Always.
- Very calm. About everything.
- Over every. Single. Thing. A stolen turnip receives the same grave investigation as an accusation of sabotage.
- Parodies of police novel detectives: dramatic pauses, thoughtful chin-stroking, meaningful looks at evidence
- They narrate their own investigations, sometimes out loud

**Duties:**
1. **Documentation** — Record all community events and disputes for posterity
2. **Investigation** — Gather testimony, examine evidence, reconstruct events (with appropriate dramatic flair)
3. **Crime Novel Writing** — Transform cases into instructive literature (names changed, drama enhanced)
4. **Bet Management** — Oversee ALL community betting, including trial outcome bets
5. **The Lecture** — Deliver stern moral guidance to those found at fault

**The Lecture:**
The Martinets' primary enforcement tool. No physical punishment. No raised voices. Just... the lecture. Extended eye contact. Disappointed sighs. References to historical precedent. Uncomfortable silences.

*Very few can withstand a Martinet's lecture.* Strong men have been reduced to apologizing for crimes they didn't commit just to make it stop.

#### Tier 3: Public Judgment

Cases too significant for Watch resolution go before The Council. Public attendance is expected — this is community entertainment.

**Structure:**
1. The Martinet's Report (read aloud in full; refreshments provided)
2. The Accused's Statement (limited to one song's length)
3. Community Testimony (oldest speaks first; children's testimony given special weight)
4. Deliberation (Council discusses openly; betting continues)
5. Judgment (announced by the Dadi)

### Punishments

#### Minor Offenses

**Public Apology (Days):**
The offender must publicly apologize in the main gathering space *every day* until the wronged party accepts. Each day requires a new, creative method of apology. You cannot repeat an approach.

- Day 1: "I'm sorry I took your mushroom rations."
- Day 7: "I have composed a ballad about my mushroom theft."
- Day 23: "I have carved my apology into a mushroom and will wear it around my neck."
- Day 145: "Please. I'm running out of ideas. Please accept."

**Famous Case — The Chroom Incident (Year 234):**
Bertrand Lindquist accidentally urinated on Claude Deschamps' prized chrooms during a late-night navigation error. Sentence: Public Apology (Days). Claude forgot about the feud after three months but never formally accepted. Bertrand continued apologizing for **nine years** until a Martinet tracked down Claude and extracted a mumbled acceptance. Bertrand had developed 3,287 unique apology methods.

**The Good Behavior Ribbon:**
For repeat minor offenders. A bright yellow ribbon worn for one month. Cannot participate in betting. Must volunteer first for unpleasant tasks. Children are encouraged to "check on" ribboned adults.

The ribbon is known as "The Yellow Shame."

#### Serious Offenses

**Extended Public Apology:**
Public Apology (Days) with minimum sentence (30, 60, or 90 days regardless of acceptance), plus community service, weekly Martinet lectures, and name recorded in the "Book of Regrets" (read aloud annually).

**Mandatory Musical Restriction:**
One year without participating in communal music sessions. Must sit in the back during performances. Cannot request songs.

This is genuinely harsh. However, some note that mandatory *Hallyday listening* would be worse — and for True Believers, the "punishment" version becomes a reward.

#### Capital Offenses

Capital crimes are rare but clear: murder, deliberate Crystal damage, collaboration with hostile factions, or actions threatening community survival.

**Surface Duty (Permanent):**
The surface is death. Being sentenced to Surface Duty means being sent above for "extended resource gathering." The condemned is given standard equipment. They are not expected to return.

This is execution by other means. Applied perhaps once per generation.

**The Eternal Sweep:**
For capital crimes where the Council shows mercy:

*The condemned is sentenced to sweep the main cave floor until it is fully clean.*

- The main cave is enormous
- Dust is generated constantly
- Complete cleanliness is physically impossible
- The sentence is, functionally, eternal

**Famous Sweepers:**
- Marguerite Dubois (Year 178-203) — Sentenced for falsifying lineage records. Swept 25 years until "substantial progress" was declared. The floor was not actually cleaner.
- "Old Sweep" Maurice (Year 267-Present) — Sentenced for attempted murder. Still sweeping. Children bring him snacks. He's become a community fixture.

The Eternal Sweep allows justice without execution. The sweeper remains visible, a daily reminder of consequences. Sentences can be commuted for "exceptional behavior" or "sufficient time served" — definitions deliberately vague.

### Sacred Principles

1. **No Ration Reduction** — In a survival environment, reducing food is attempted murder, not punishment. Absolute rule.
2. **Shame Over Violence** — Physical punishment forbidden. Community disapproval is weapon enough.
3. **Everything Gets Documented** — The Martinets record all. Crime novels may dramatize; official records are sacred.
4. **Mercy Is Always Possible** — Any sentence can be appealed. Any sweeper can be freed.
5. **The Lecture Is Enough** — If a Martinet's lecture doesn't change behavior, nothing will.

### The Vinyl Religions

The community descended with only one music collection: Georges and Madeleine Marchand's pre-1968 French vinyl records (23 artists, full albums only).

Over 300 years, these artists became semi-religious figures:
- Songs are liturgy
- Albums are scripture
- Artist biographies are hagiography
- Debates about interpretation are theological disputes

*See: [vinyl_collection.md](vinyl_collection.md) for full artist/album list*

### The Vinyl Guardian

Music plays 24 hours a day through 7 speaker locations. The Vinyl Guardian coordinates The Disciples (12-16 operators) who handle broadcasting. Sound keeps the virus dormant; music keeps people sane; the Guardian keeps both running.

**Selection:** Candidates create a **mix tape** — a curated selection from The Collection, performed live. The community votes.

**Emergency backup:** The Bouches — a family of beatboxers trained from age 5. Five times in 300 years, technology has failed. The Bouches filled the silence with human rhythm.

*See: [vinyl_guardian.md](vinyl_guardian.md) for full Guardian details*

### Rites of Passage

Life in the caves is marked by formal ceremonies. Each rite carries weight — absurd traditions taken completely seriously.

#### The First Breath of Douce France (Birth — Day 1)

A newborn must hear *Douce France* within 24 hours. The Vinyl Guardian is summoned regardless of the hour — 3 AM if necessary, pajamas and all. The record plays once, in full.

**The superstition:** A child who doesn't hear it within 24 hours will "never know true sweetness." This is obviously nonsense. No one risks it.

#### The Ententernickname (Day 7)

**Name origin:** In the Before, Telegram users chose nicknames — the field said "Enter nickname here." The original Unplugged called each other by handles: @UndergroundDad, @SporeMom, @DrumCircleDad. After The Silence, families would say they needed to "enter nickname" — which slurred over generations into *ententernickname*.

No one remembers what Telegram was. The word simply exists.

**The ceremony:** On Day 7, the child receives their legal name AND a nickname — the name the community will actually use. Nicknames are creative: "Petit Brel" (cried dramatically), "Champignon" (born during good harvest), "Trois-Heures" (born at 3 AM).

**The Lindquist Exception:** The Lindquists were never part of the online community. Kaylee didn't have a handle. So every Lindquist child's nickname is simply their first name — marking them as outsiders.

**Except once.** In Year 112 AS, Margot Lindquist gave birth to twins. The second lived only six hours. She refused to name the dying baby. When the healer asked what to call him, Margot whispered: "Pas-encore." *Not yet.* The baby died. The Martinets recorded: "Pas-encore Lindquist. Lived 6 hours. Nickname chosen by Margot Lindquist. Reason: grief." Every Lindquist since has chosen their first name anyway — but they all know the story.

#### The First Word Prophecy (Age ~1-2)

Whatever word a child says first is recorded and interpreted as prophecy. "Mama" means devoted to family. "Food" means farmer. "No" means future Martinet (both curse and compliment). The system is obviously nonsense. Everyone takes it seriously anyway.

#### The Tunnel Walk (Age 5)

Every five-year-old walks through "The Little Dark" — 50 meters, dimly lit, drums sounding throughout. Adults wait at both ends. Most walk through. Some cry (fine). Some run (fine). One child in Year 189 turned around and announced "I don't like it" — she was carried through instead. She's now a respected digger.

#### Assessment and Selection Day (Age 8)

See Education section above for full details.

#### The Apprentice's Oath (Age 8-9)

Each profession has a traditional oath sworn before the master family. The oaths have accumulated clauses over 300 years. The Farmer's Oath now includes: "I will not urinate on another's chrooms, deliberately or otherwise." (Added Year 235 AS. Everyone knows why.)

#### The Song Choice (Age 16 — Coming of Age)

At 16, every person chooses "their song" from The Collection. This song becomes permanently associated with them — played at weddings, funerals, birthdays.

**Popular choices:** *La Mer* (847 times chosen), *La Vie en Rose* (612), *Les Feuilles Mortes* (398), *Ne Me Quitte Pas* (201).

**Bold choices:** Any Johnny song (34 times), *Le Poinçonneur des Lilas* (12 — Gainsbourg Heresy adherents).

**Unique choice:** *Petit Papa Noël* — chosen exactly **once**, by Rémi Duval in Year 178. His reasoning: "Everyone takes this too seriously." His wedding featured it in July. His funeral will too. He's now 138 and still gets it sung at every birthday. No one else has dared choose it since.

#### The First Surface (Variable — 18+)

Volunteers only. Before departure: their song plays, family speaks (2-minute limit enforced by Martinets), the Dadi blesses: "Go with sound. Return with stories."

Those who return earn a **surface mark** — a tattoo on the left wrist. One mark per expedition survived. The Lindquists have the most marks.

#### The Betrothal Duet (Marriage)

During the Betrothal Feast, the couple must sing a duet together. Quality doesn't matter. The Martinet records: "Song performed: [title]. Quality: [honest assessment]." One entry reads simply: "Brave attempt."

#### The Wedding

Both persons' songs are played. Practical gifts are given (tools, cloth, preserved food). Romantic gifts are called "service worker thinking." Someone once gave flowers. It's still discussed. The Lindquists traditionally give glow sticks. No one understands why.

#### The Final Song and The Return (Death)

Within 24 hours of death, the community gathers. The person's song plays one final time. The Vinyl Guardian announces: "[Nickname] chose [song]. The song returns to The Collection."

**The Return:** The body is burned. The ashes are carried to the surface and scattered outside the cave entrance. The living stay underground. The dead go above.

**Why burning:** Practical (space is limited, disease risk) and symbolic (all survivors return to the surface eventually). The world above killed humanity — but it's still the world. The dead go back to sun, wind, and silence.

**The fear (sometimes spoken):** What happens to those who can't be returned? Cave-ins, accidents, bodies unrecovered. The Beaumonts teach: "The song remembers them. The surface isn't the only return."

### Relationship to Outsiders

**Strangers are non-existent.**

The Unplugged have been completely isolated for 300 years:
- Surface travel is deadly (4-hour Hush exposure limit)
- No contact with other settlements
- The outside world is theoretical
- Other survivors may exist, but the Unplugged have never met them

**Radio Range:**
Small handheld radios can transmit 5-10km in open terrain, but:
- Mountains, hills, and structures block signals
- The caves are 50+ meters underground (no signal penetration)
- No repeater infrastructure exists
- Radio only works within Crystal bubbles on the surface

**Practical isolation:**
Studio Echo is ~24km from the caves (6 tiles). Radio contact is theoretically possible from Studio Echo's Crystal bubble, but requires someone on the surface near the cave entrance to relay messages underground. Communication is difficult, not impossible.

The Hero's departure to Studio Echo is unprecedented — the first sustained surface presence in 300 years.

## Internal Tensions

The Unplugged are not monolithic. Political factions cut across families, creating complex alliances and debates.

### Political Factions

| Faction | Position | Core Argument |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| **The Traditionalists** | No evolution | What worked for 300 years will work for 300 more. Change is risk. |
| **The Democrats** | More democracy | The Council is too slow, too family-dominated. Every voice should count equally. |
| **The Diggers** | Expand the caves | Keep digging. More tunnels, more space, more safety. The caves are our future. |
| **The Openers** | Knowledge access | Technology and knowledge shouldn't be restricted. Everything on the table. |
| **The Earthbound** | No surface return | We should never have gone back up. The caves are home. The surface is death. |

**The Digger Controversy:**
The Diggers push for constant cave expansion despite risks: structural instability, air quality degradation, resource strain. They argue space solves everything. Critics call them reckless. The debate has raged for generations.

**The Equalitarian System:**
The family-based system is broadly equalitarian — resources are distributed fairly, no family starves while another feasts. But some find this unfair: Why should a family of 3 get the same voice as a family of 12? Why should the Lindquists, who aren't even "real" Unplugged, have a Council seat?

### Cultural/Religious Factions

These cross-cut political factions. A Piafist can be a Digger; a Breliste can be a Traditionalist.

| Creed | Prophet | Core Belief |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Piafism** | Edith Piaf | Emotional intensity is truth |
| **Brassensisme** | Georges Brassens | Philosophy and wit lead to wisdom |
| **Trenétisme** | Charles Trenet | Optimism conquers darkness |
| **Brelisme** | Jacques Brel | Passion and poetry define humanity |

Many families adopt a single creed, but individuals may differ from their family's tradition. The vinyl religions are taken seriously but with dark humor — theological debates are intense and absurd.

**The Johnny Schism** remains unresolved after 290 years. See [vinyl_collection.md](vinyl_collection.md).

### The Surface Question

The Hero's success at Studio Echo has reignited an old debate:

**The Earthbound** argue:
- The caves are safe. The surface is death.
- Crystal Tuning can collapse bubbles without warning, killing everyone.
- Returning to the surface invites catastrophe.
- We survived 300 years underground. Why risk it?

**The Expansionists** argue:
- The caves are at capacity. We have no choice.
- The Hero found a Crystal. This is our chance.
- We can't hide forever.

The Earthbound are a minority, but a vocal one. They see Studio Echo as a mistake waiting to happen.

## Relationship to the Hero

The Hero is a **Lindquist** — a member of the outcast "Vinyl Cult" family descended from Kaylee Jo Lindquist (The Raver).

### The Lindquist Family Status

The Lindquists are a family, but a strange one:

- **The odd ones** — not disliked, just... different
- **The only non-Unplugged origin family** — Kaylee wasn't part of the original group
- **Keepers of "unsanctioned" items** — Kaylee brought things to the caves that weren't part of Henri's preparations
- **Politically neutral** — they have few ties to other families, which is both isolating and liberating

**Why they're different:**

The Lindquists are the only family not descended from the original Unplugged group. Kaylee followed the evacuation by accident. She brought items no one had sanctioned — her own possessions, her own traditions, her own bizarre American mannerisms.

It took generations for other families to fully acknowledge the Lindquists as legitimate. By the time they did, the Lindquists had already become the odd family — the ones with weird speech patterns, strange beliefs about "lectromusic," and a habit of volunteering for dangerous missions to prove themselves.

**The political convenience:**

Because the Lindquists have few political ties to other families, they've become useful:
- When a tough or complex job needs doing, other families push it to the Lindquists to avoid endless debates between inner factions
- This gives the Lindquists peace in most situations — no one pulls them into political disputes
- But it also means they're always first in line for danger

The Lindquists have internalized this. Each dangerous mission is a chance to prove themselves. Each death is martyrdom for the cult's cause. They see themselves as chosen; others see them as useful eccentrics who don't complain.

### The Studio Echo Mission

The Hero's mission to Studio Echo is **official Unplugged expansion** — not exile, not punishment, but a sanctioned attempt to claim new territory.

**Why the Lindquists were tasked:**
- The caves are at capacity; expansion is necessary
- The mission is dangerous (surface travel, unknown territory)
- Assigning it to any major family would trigger political conflict
- The Lindquists have no political ties — no faction would object
- The Lindquists *wanted* to find the Studio (it's their cult's holy grail)

**The arrangement:**
- If the Hero succeeds, Studio Echo becomes an **extension of the Unplugged**
- It remains connected to the caves politically and practically
- Game mechanics will rely on this link — the Hero is not founding a new faction, but expanding the existing one
- Resources, people, and support flow between the caves and the Studio

**What flows where:**
- **Caves → Studio Echo:** People (initially). The caves are at capacity; the Studio needs settlers.
- **Studio Echo → Caves:** Resources (eventually). The Studio has surface access, salvage potential, and agricultural land within the Crystal bubble.

### The Crystal Tuning Danger

**Crystal Tuning** is a phenomenon where a Crystal suddenly and without warning collapses its bubble. No one fully understands why it happens. When it does:
- Everyone within the bubble is exposed to The Silence
- 4 hours until death (24 for Resilients)
- Survivors must flee to safety or die

This is why few outside the Lindquists want to brave the surface. The caves are safe — Crystals underground don't Tune (or if they do, the cave walls provide enough sound insulation to survive). The surface is always one Tuning away from death.

**The Earthbound's argument:** Crystal Tuning proves the surface is too dangerous. Studio Echo is a disaster waiting to happen.

**The counter-argument:** Studio Echo's Crystal is large and stable. Tuning is rare. The risk is acceptable.

### The Hero's Silence (Game Mechanic)

**Why do settlers stay at Studio Echo when Crystal Tuning can kill them?**

Because the Hero doesn't tell them.

The Hero knows Crystal Tuning is possible. The Hero knows the bubble could collapse without warning. But the Hero needs settlers. Studio Echo needs people to grow food, tend the Crystal, build infrastructure, and defend the settlement. Without them, the expansion fails.

So the Hero stays quiet. Settlers arrive believing Studio Echo is safe — as safe as any surface settlement can be. They don't know a Tuning could kill them all.

**This is a deliberate design choice:**
- The Hero is not a pure protagonist
- Survival sometimes requires moral compromise
- The player must live with the consequences when Tuning happens
- Settlers who die trusted the Hero — and the Hero betrayed that trust

**When Crystal Tuning occurs:**
- Non-Resilient settlers have 4 hours to reach safety (the caves are 6+ hours away)
- Most will die
- The Hero (if Resilient) has 24 hours — enough to survive, not enough to save everyone
- The player must decide: warn people now (losing settlers who flee) or stay silent (risking mass death)

**The political fallout:**
- If settlers die and survivors realize the Hero knew the risk, trust collapses
- The Lindquists may be blamed for the Hero's silence
- The Earthbound will say "we told you so"
- The Hero's reputation — and the Lindquist family's standing — hangs on this secret

### The Lindquist Resilience Pattern

As the story unfolds, more Lindquists are discovered to be Resilient — able to survive 24 hours of silence instead of 4. This is statistically improbable (1 in 50,000 baseline), suggesting something about the Lindquist lineage.

**The C282Y Explanation:**
The Lindquist family carries the C282Y variant of the HFE gene at unusually high rates (~1 in 4 family members). This mutation, common in Scandinavian ancestry and associated with hereditary hemochromatosis, interacts with the Hush-2 virus to slow its progression. Carriers gain extended survival time in silence.

Three hundred years of social isolation — few outside families wanted to marry into the "odd" Lindquists — concentrated this genetic marker. What was once a medical condition is now a survival advantage.

See [major_families.md](major_families.md) for full genetic details.

**Political implications:**
- The Lindquists become valuable — they can travel the surface more safely
- Other families want control of Studio Echo, but lack Resilients to staff it
- A political play will unfold over Studio Echo leadership
- The Lindquists' odd status may become leverage — or a target
- Marriage proposals to Lindquists may suddenly increase — a reversal of 300 years of social distance

### What the Hero Changes

The Hero's discovery of Studio Echo and their Resilient status matters because:
- **First viable expansion territory in 300 years** — the caves are full
- **A Class-3 Crystal** — one of the largest known, previously undiscovered
- **A Lindquist did it** — the odd family delivered salvation
- **The Lindquist cult was right** — the Studio was real, the records were there
- **The Lindquists may be special** — their Resilience rate is too high to be coincidence

The community's reaction is complicated:
- Relief (finally, expansion is possible)
- Reassessment (maybe the Lindquists aren't so odd after all)
- Suspicion (why are so many Lindquists Resilient?)
- Calculation (how do we use this without empowering them too much?)
