# Le Grand Nord ("The Great North")

> **Quick Reference:** The union heartland — train station workers who preserved French labor culture with religious intensity.
>
> **Territory:** North — Saint-Lazare → Gare du Nord → Gare de l'Est → République
> **Population:** ~22,000-26,000
> **Government:** La Confédération (union federation)
> **Founded:** Year 5 AS (from SNCF/RATP worker coalitions)
> **Year 300 Status:** Largest faction, union-controlled, frequently on strike

---

## Origin Story

### Day 1: The Workers Were Ready

At 5:34 AM on October 14, 2034, the northern train stations were waking up.

**Gare du Nord** — France's busiest station. Early Eurostar departures, commuter trains, metro connections. Staff already working.

**Gare de l'Est** — Regional trains east. Maintenance crews on night shift.

**Saint-Lazare** — Western suburbs commuter hub. First wave of morning workers arriving.

**Who was there:**
- ~45,000 RATP/SNCF employees across Paris (many at northern stations)
- Early commuters (~10,000)
- Night shift workers going home
- Station businesses (cafes, kiosks) opening
- Homeless sheltering

When The Silence hit, the railway workers had advantages:
- Organization (unions already existed)
- Communication (internal networks, radios)
- Infrastructure knowledge (they built this)
- Emergency protocols (they'd trained for crises)

### The Union Response (Day 1-7)

Within hours, the railway workers did what unions do: they organized.

**Day 1:**
- Shop stewards called emergency assemblies
- Workers secured station areas
- Resources inventoried
- "Emergency Safety Committee" formed (a union committee, of course)

**Day 3:**
- Formal coalition between SNCF (national railway) and RATP (metro) workers
- First resource-sharing agreement
- Security perimeter established

**Day 7:**
- "La Confédération du Nord" declared
- Union bylaws adapted for survival
- First formal election (for Emergency Secretary General)

While other areas descended into chaos, Le Grand Nord held democratic votes.

### Year 1-10: Consolidation

**The Union Advantage:**

French railway workers had been striking, organizing, and fighting management for a century. Survival was just another labor dispute.

**What they preserved:**
- The 35-hour work week (constitutional, non-negotiable)
- Collective bargaining (for ration allocation)
- Strike rights (yes, even during crises)
- Shop steward representation
- The sacred déjeuner (lunch break, 12:00-14:00)

**What changed:**
- "Management" became "the crisis"
- "Wages" became "rations"
- "Working conditions" became "survival"
- The fight continued

### The Great War (Year 78-82)

When La Dalle Éternelle tried to seize Le Carrefour, Le Grand Nord saw it for what it was: corporate aggression against the working class.

**Le Grand Nord's response:**
- Formed defensive coalition with L'Est Profond
- Blockaded supply routes
- Called a general strike
- Then went back on strike against the war

**The Great Strike of Year 79:**

In the middle of the war, Le Grand Nord's workers voted to strike.

Against both sides.

For three weeks, they refused to fight, refused to work, refused to participate in the war at all. Both sides were furious. Neither could force them back.

The strike's demands:
1. Immediate ceasefire
2. Recognition of labor rights across all factions
3. A 15% increase in rest periods during wartime

They got the ceasefire. The war ended shortly after.

## Year 300: The Workers' Republic

### What They Became

Le Grand Nord is the largest faction in Paris — and the most stubbornly ideological. They preserved French labor culture with religious intensity. Some call it socialism. They call it "basic dignity."

### Governance: La Confédération

**Structure:**
Everything is union-organized. Every job has a union. Every union has delegates. Delegates form councils. Councils form La Confédération.

**Key Bodies:**

| Body | Role | Members |
|------|------|---------|
| **L'Assemblée Syndicale** | Legislature | All union delegates (~300) |
| **Le Comité Exécutif** | Executive | 15 elected union leaders |
| **Le Secrétaire Général** | Leader (theoretically) | 1, elected every 4 years |
| **La Commission de Travail** | Work allocation | Union representatives |
| **Le Tribunal du Travail** | Labor disputes | Elected judges |

**The Secrétaire Général:**

Officially, the highest authority. In practice, constantly hamstrung by unions.

The current Secrétaire, **Françoise Martin**, has been in office for 8 years. She's popular. Her approval rating is 67%. She still can't pass anything the unions don't like.

**Decision-making:**
1. Someone proposes something
2. Unions debate it
3. Unions vote on whether to debate it further
4. Unions form committees to study it
5. Committees report back
6. Unions vote
7. If passed, implementation requires union approval
8. Implementation may trigger strike action

Average time to pass legislation: 8 months.

### Union Structure

**Major Unions:**

| Union | Membership | Power |
|-------|------------|-------|
| **Syndicat des Cheminots** | Railway workers (legacy) | Highest |
| **Syndicat des Souterrains** | Tunnel workers | High |
| **Syndicat de l'Agriculture** | Mushroom farmers | Medium-High |
| **Syndicat de la Sécurité** | Guards, defense | Medium |
| **Syndicat des Services** | General services | Medium |
| **Syndicat de la Santé** | Healthcare | Medium |
| **Syndicat de l'Éducation** | Teachers | Medium |
| **Syndicat de la Culture** | Artists, performers | Lower |

**Union membership:**
- Mandatory for employment
- Dues: 5% of ration allocation
- Benefits: Representation, strike pay, mutual aid

**Shop stewards (Délégués Syndicaux):**
Every workplace has them. They represent workers, negotiate conditions, file grievances. The backbone of governance.

## Daily Life

### The Work Week

**35 hours. Non-negotiable.**

Le Grand Nord negotiated it down to 32 in Year 156. The Cheminots union held out for 30 but compromised.

**Standard schedule:**
- 7 hours/day, 5 days/week
- OR 8 hours/day, 4 days/week (popular option)
- Overtime: Rare, requires union approval, paid at 150%
- Night shifts: Premium allocation

**Rest periods:**
- **Le Déjeuner (12:00-14:00):** Sacred. The world stops. No work, no meetings, no exceptions.
- **Coffee breaks:** 15 minutes, twice daily, constitutionally protected
- **Annual leave:** 5 weeks minimum

### Strikes

Strikes are legal, frequent, and celebrated.

**Strike types:**

| Type | Description | Frequency |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Grève générale** | General strike, all workers | Rare (major crises) |
| **Grève sectorielle** | One sector strikes | Monthly |
| **Grève de solidarité** | Supporting another union | Frequent |
| **Grève perlée** | Slowdown (work-to-rule) | Common |
| **Débrayage** | Brief walkout | Weekly |

**Strike rules:**
- 48-hour notice required (préavis de grève)
- Strike fund pays workers during strikes
- Scabs (briseurs de grève) are socially destroyed
- Management cannot fire strikers

**Notable strikes:**
- **The Great Strike of Year 79:** Against the war itself
- **The Lunch Strike of Year 134:** When someone suggested shortening déjeuner
- **The Strike of Nothing (Year 201):** Workers struck against a policy that hadn't been proposed yet (preemptive strike)
- **The Strike Strike (Year 267):** A union struck against another union's strike (union rivalry)

### Retirement

**Retirement age: 55.**

Underground. With no surface to retire to. But the principle matters.

**Retirees receive:**
- Full ration allocation
- Priority housing
- "Elder status" in union assemblies
- The right to complain about younger workers (exercised constantly)

### The Trains

**The trains don't run. But the schedules exist.**

In every station, hand-painted schedules are posted:
- 06:47 — TGV to Lyon (Cancelled — 300 years running)
- 07:12 — RER B to CDG Airport (Cancelled)
- 08:03 — Intercités to Strasbourg (Cancelled)

Nobody knows why they maintain the schedules. Nobody questions it. It's tradition.

**The announcement system:**
Pre-Silence announcement recordings (restored from memory) play at scheduled times.
"Le train à destination de Marseille Saint-Charles va partir voie 7."
The train will never depart. But the announcement plays. It's comforting.

## Culture & Customs

### The Labor Faith

Work is dignity. Unions are salvation. Management is the enemy (even when there's no management).

**Core beliefs:**
- Labor creates value
- Workers deserve the fruits of their labor
- Collective action is righteous
- The strike is sacred

**Rituals:**
- **La Fête du Travail (May 1):** Major holiday, parades, speeches, no work
- **L'Assemblée Générale:** Regular mass meetings, democratic participation
- **Le Piquet de Grève:** The strike picket, a place of honor
- **La Remise des Cartes:** Union card ceremony for new members

### Language

Standard French with heavy union jargon:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Camarade** | Comrade (universal greeting) |
| **La base** | The ordinary workers |
| **La direction** | Whoever's in charge (enemy by default) |
| **Acquis sociaux** | Hard-won rights (sacred) |
| **Rapport de force** | Balance of power |
| **Mot d'ordre** | Official position |

**Greetings:**
- "Salut camarade" — Hello comrade
- "Solidairement" — In solidarity (goodbye)
- "À la lutte" — To the struggle (goodbye, more militant)

### Entertainment

**Les Cabarets Ouvriers (Workers' Cabarets):**
Performance venues featuring:
- Political songs
- Satirical comedy
- Poetry readings
- The occasional actual entertainment

**Les Choeurs du Nord (Northern Choirs):**
Massive group singing. Hundreds of voices. Union songs, folk songs, revolutionary songs.

**Le Théâtre Populaire:**
Free performances. Heavily subsidized. Mandatory attendance at union-sponsored shows.

### Food

Station cafeterias serve all. Food is distributed by union allocation.

**Menu (typical):**
- Mushroom soup (always)
- Mushroom steak (often)
- Mushroom bread (daily)
- "Coffee" (mushroom-based, terrible, traditional)
- Wine (mushroom-fermented, actually drinkable)

**Dining customs:**
- Lunch is communal (cafeteria seating)
- Hierarchy exists but is subtle
- Complaining about the food is traditional
- Complimenting the food is suspicious

## Military & Defense

### The Militancy Question

Le Grand Nord doesn't have a "military." They have "organized workers' defense."

**Structure:**
The Syndicat de la Sécurité provides guards, patrols, and fighters when needed.

- ~1,500 "security workers"
- Organized like any other union
- Strikes during wartime are possible (see: Year 79)
- Officers are elected by the guards themselves

**Weapons:**
- Batons (standard)
- Crossbows (border patrols)
- Railway equipment repurposed (heavy tools, pry bars, etc.)
- The occasional firearm (rare, legacy items)

**Defense strategy:**
The train stations are fortresses. Narrow platforms, limited approaches, easy to defend. Le Grand Nord has held their territory since Year 5.

### Historical Conflicts

**The Great War (Year 78-82):**
Fought against La Dalle coalition. Ended via strike action. Le Grand Nord considers this a victory.

**The Eastern Skirmishes (Year 203-207):**
Border disputes with L'Est Profond over République station. Resolved through "solidarity negotiations" (L'Est Profond joined some Le Grand Nord unions).

**The Catacomb Incident (Year 289):**
Le Grand Nord raiders entered Les Ossuaires territory. They didn't return. Le Grand Nord filed a formal complaint. Les Ossuaires said nothing.

### Currency

**Union Scrip:**
Internal currency issued by La Confédération.

- Backed by labor-hours
- Exchangeable for goods at union stores
- Convertible at Le Carrefour (fluctuating rate)

### Trade Relations

| Faction | Status | Trade |
|---------|--------|-------|
| La Dalle | Hostile | Minimal, through Le Carrefour |
| Le Carrefour | Neutral | Major trade partner |
| L'Est Profond | Friendly | Extensive, some union overlap |
| Les Ossuaires | Cold | Limited, after Year 289 |
| Le Palais Profond | Polite | Occasional, formal |
| Les Canaux | Useful | Passage and smuggling |

### Labor Export

Le Grand Nord's biggest export is workers.

Other factions hire Le Grand Nord laborers for:
- Construction
- Heavy work
- Technical skills
- Union organizing (not always welcome)

**Rules:**
- Workers must maintain union membership
- Dues still paid
- Working conditions negotiated
- Exploitation reported and addressed

## Key Locations

### Gare du Nord

The heart of Le Grand Nord. Former Eurostar terminal.

**Features:**
- La Confédération headquarters
- Main assembly hall
- The "Platform 1" — ceremonial space for major announcements
- Archives (union records, pre-Silence schedules)

### Gare de l'Est

Secondary hub. Regional connections.

**Features:**
- Workshops (metalworking, tool production)
- Training facilities
- Union school

### Saint-Lazare

Western gateway. Connections to La Dalle territory (heavily guarded border).

**Features:**
- Border checkpoints
- Market area (smaller than Le Carrefour)
- The "Militant's Corner" — statue/shrine to historical union leaders

### République

Eastern boundary. Contested with L'Est Profond, now shared.

**Features:**
- Cultural exchange zone
- Joint union offices
- The infamous "République Canteen" — fusion cuisine (controversial)

### The Ministry of Labor Relations

**One person. On permanent strike.**

Officially, this office coordinates labor policy across factions. In practice, it's occupied by a single individual who refuses to work (as a protest against... something. No one remembers what).

They're paid. They do nothing. It's respected.

## Notable Figures

### Secrétaire Générale Françoise Martin

52 years old. Leader of La Confédération.

**Background:** Rose through Syndicat des Cheminots. Daughter of a rail worker, granddaughter of a rail worker. Third-generation union.

**Personality:** Charismatic, pragmatic, exhausted. Genuinely believes in worker solidarity. Also knows how to compromise (which makes radicals suspicious).

**Known for:**
- The "Martin Accords" (Year 293) — trade normalization with L'Est Profond
- Never missing a union assembly
- The famous quote: "The revolution will be negotiated."

### Jean-Claude Verne (Union Firebrand)

67 years old. Leader of the Syndicat des Souterrains.

**Background:** Lifelong militant. Has called more strikes than anyone living.

**Personality:** Ideological, uncompromising, inspiring. Believes the revolution is still coming. Has been saying this for 45 years.

**Known for:**
- The "Verne Doctrine" — no compromise with capital (meaning La Dalle)
- Leading the Year 267 Strike Strike
- Referring to everyone as "camarade" including enemies

### Dr. Marie Dubois (Healthcare Union)

48 years old. Head of Syndicat de la Santé.

**Background:** Doctor, union organizer, healthcare advocate.

**Personality:** Practical, caring, politically savvy. Uses healthcare access as political leverage. Everyone needs doctors.

**Known for:**
- Universal healthcare in Le Grand Nord
- "The Dubois Protocol" — medical care even for enemies
- Winning every negotiation by threatening doctor strikes

### "The Railway Ghost"

Age unknown. Identity unknown. Possibly mythical.

**Legend:** A figure seen walking the abandoned upper platforms at night. Wears pre-Silence railway uniform. Checks tickets that don't exist. Announces trains that never come.

**Reality:** Unknown. Some say it's real. Some say it's collective delusion. Some say it's a very dedicated performance artist.

**Known for:**
- Appearances during crises
- Never speaking
- Vanishing when approached

## Absurdist Details

### Things That Are Completely Normal

1. **Strike notices for personal disputes.** "I hereby give 48-hour notice of strike action against my husband for failure to complete domestic duties." This is legally binding.

2. **The trains don't run, but the schedules exist.** Updated annually. Argued over extensively. The 07:12 to CDG is always late (conceptually).

3. **Retirement at 55 underground.** Where do you retire to? Nowhere. But you deserve rest. Workers die at 70 knowing their last 15 years were their own.

4. **Coffee breaks are constitutional.** Seriously. It's in the founding documents. Removing coffee breaks requires 2/3 majority AND referendum.

5. **They went on strike during the Great War.** Against both sides. For rest periods. And won.

6. **The Ministry of Labor Relations is one person who doesn't work.** Nobody questions this. It's protest art. Or commitment. Or both.

7. **There's a union for unionizers.** The "Syndicat des Délégués" — a union for shop stewards. They've struck against other unions. Recursively.

8. **Lunch is sacred.** In Year 134, someone suggested shortening déjeuner by 15 minutes. The general strike lasted 3 weeks. No one has suggested it since.

## Relations with Other Factions

### La Dalle Éternelle (Hostile)

The ideological enemy. Corporate vs. Labor. The class war continues underground.

**History:**
- Great War allies against each other
- Trade embargoes (regular)
- Propaganda wars
- Workers from La Dalle have defected to Le Grand Nord (celebrated)

**Current status:** Cold war. Trade through Le Carrefour only. Occasional escalations.

### L'Est Profond (Friendly)

Fellow workers, diverse traditions, shared values.

**History:**
- Alliance in Great War
- Border tensions at République (resolved)
- Some L'Est Profond workers joined Le Grand Nord unions

**Current status:** Good relations. Cultural exchange. Joint holidays.

### Le Carrefour (Necessary)

Le Grand Nord needs Le Carrefour for trade. They don't love it.

**Current status:** Pragmatic cooperation. Le Grand Nord has a Tribunal seat.

### Les Ossuaires (Cold)

The Year 289 incident remains unresolved. Le Grand Nord raiders died in catacomb ambush.

**Current status:** Minimal contact. Mutual suspicion.

### Le Palais Profond (Polite Disdain)

Le Grand Nord doesn't recognize bourgeois republics. But they'll negotiate.

**Current status:** Formal relations. Trade happens. No warmth.

### Les Canaux (Useful)

Les Canaux provide services. Le Grand Nord pays for them.

**Current status:** Transactional. Les Canaux workers have unsuccessfully tried to form a union (complicated jurisdictional issue).

## Quest Hooks

### The Scab Crisis

**Hook:** Workers from another faction are being hired to undermine Le Grand Nord labor. Union leadership is furious.

**Reality:** La Dalle is paying premium rates for workers willing to break strikes.

**Implications:** Could escalate to open conflict. Needs diplomatic or covert resolution.

### The Railway Ghost Investigation

**Hook:** The Railway Ghost has been seen more frequently. Some say it's an omen.

**Reality:** Unknown. Investigate to find out.

**Implications:** Could be natural, supernatural, or a very dedicated person with access to pre-Silence uniforms.

### The Lost Archives

**Hook:** Pre-Silence union records exist somewhere in the deep stations. They contain information about original agreements.

**Reality:** The records are real. They might change current power dynamics.

**Implications:** Finding them could resolve old disputes — or start new ones.

### The Cross-Faction Strike

**Hook:** Workers across multiple factions are organizing. They want a Paris-wide general strike.

**Reality:** This has never happened. It could unite the underground — or tear it apart.

**Implications:** Le Grand Nord leadership is split. Some support it. Some fear losing control.

### The Year 289 Truth

**Hook:** A survivor from the catacomb incident returns. They know what really happened.

**Reality:** The raiders weren't killed. They found something. They were... changed.

**Implications:** Reopens wounds with Les Ossuaires. What did they find?
