## Overview

The Vinyl Guardian is not a librarian. The Vinyl Guardian is not a DJ. The Vinyl Guardian is the person responsible for ensuring that **music never stops**.

In the caves of Les Grottes de la Bresme, sound keeps the Hush-2 virus dormant. The drums provide continuous rhythm, but music — broadcast through the cave system via salvaged speakers — is the soul of the community. The Guardian coordinates The Disciples, manages The Collection, and ensures that every corner of the caves receives sound.

If the music stops, people die.

The position is sacred.

## Selection Process

### The Mix Tape Competition

Every year, candidates for Vinyl Guardian must create a **mix tape** — a curated selection of songs from The Collection, arranged in a specific order, with spoken introductions explaining their choices.

**The Rules:**
- Any adult (16+) may submit a mix tape
- Mix tapes must be between 45 minutes and 2 hours
- Candidates must perform their mix tape live, using the record player
- Transitions between songs are judged (smooth changes earn respect)
- Commentary between songs is expected (why this song? why this order?)

**The Vote:**
After all candidates have performed (usually over 2-3 days), the community votes. Each person gets one vote. Simple majority wins.

**Campaign Promises:**
Mix tape competitions have become political events. Candidates make promises:
- "More morning Trenet, less evening Brel"
- "Fairer distribution of Johnny across time slots"
- "I will finally add variety to the 3 AM rotation"
- "Aznavour at dinner, every night"

**Notable Elections:**
- **Year 45 AS:** The first contested election. Marc Delacroix retired from the position; three candidates competed. Winner: Thérèse Moreau, who promised "military precision in broadcast schedules."
- **Year 156 AS:** The same year as the Tino Rossi Protocols. The winning candidate ran on a platform of "seasonal music discipline."
- **Year 234 AS:** A Lindquist ran for the first time. They lost, but earned respect for including a 30-second "moment of imagination" where they hummed what they called "lectromusic." Some found it moving. Others found it disturbing.
- **Year 299 AS:** The current Guardian, [NAME TBD], won with a mix tape featuring bold Johnny placement at 7 PM — prime listening hours.

## The Disciples

The Vinyl Guardian doesn't work alone. They lead **The Disciples** — a rotating team of 12-16 trained operators who handle the actual broadcasting.

### Structure

| Role | Number | Duty |
|------|--------|------|
| **Guardian** | 1 | Overall coordination, scheduling, disputes, needle management |
| **Lead Disciples** | 3 | Shift supervisors (8-hour shifts) |
| **Disciples** | 9-12 | Record operation, speaker monitoring, maintenance |
| **Apprentices** | Variable | Training positions (children 12-16) |

### The Broadcast System

**Primary Station:** La Salle Piaf — the music room where The Collection lives

**Distribution:**
- 7 speaker locations throughout the caves
- Wired connections (salvaged copper, maintained religiously)
- Volume adjusted per location (louder in work areas, softer in sleeping quarters)
- Backup hand-crank players at 2 secondary locations

**The Schedule:**
Music runs 24 hours, but volume and selection vary:
- **6 AM - 12 PM:** Energetic (Trenet, upbeat Piaf, acceptable Johnny)
- **12 PM - 6 PM:** Varied (rotates based on Guardian's weekly plan)
- **6 PM - 10 PM:** Evening (Brassens, contemplative Brel, community favorites)
- **10 PM - 6 AM:** Low volume (gentle selections, nothing jarring)

The Guardian publishes weekly schedules. Requests are accepted but not guaranteed.

## Sacred Duties

### 1. The Sound Never Stops

The Guardian's primary duty. Music must play continuously. If a record ends, another must begin within seconds. If the player fails, backup systems activate. If all fails, **The Bouches** are summoned.

### 2. Needle Management

The 7 remaining needles are the community's most critical resource. The Guardian:
- Tracks needle wear (estimated plays remaining)
- Decides when to change needles
- Performs the needle-changing ritual
- Guards the Needle Cabinet (locked, inventoried weekly)

### 3. The Collection Inventory

Weekly inventory of all 312 playable records. Condition noted. Scratches documented. Any damage investigated.

### 4. Dispute Resolution

When families argue about scheduling, the Guardian mediates. Common disputes:
- "Why is there so much Brel at 8 PM?"
- "The Piafistes are getting too many morning slots"
- "Someone keeps requesting Johnny during children's hours"

The Guardian's decision is final. Appeals go to the Council only for major disputes.

### 5. The Tino Rossi Protocols

*Petit Papa Noël* and other Christmas music: December only. The Guardian enforces this absolutely.

### 6. Emergency Response

When something goes wrong, the Guardian coordinates response:
- Player malfunction → Switch to backup
- Speaker failure → Reroute sound
- Complete system failure → Summon The Bouches

## The Needle-Changing Ritual

When a needle must be changed, the community gathers.

**The Ceremony:**

1. **Announcement:** The Guardian declares "The needle is tired" over the broadcast system
2. **The Vigil:** One hour of continuous music — a farewell to the outgoing needle
3. **Final Song:** Voted on by those present (usually something meaningful)
4. **The Silence:** A terrifying 30-60 seconds while the Guardian works. The Bouches stand ready.
5. **First Song:** The new needle's inaugural song — traditionally *Douce France*
6. **The Record:** The old needle goes to the Girard archives with a full service record (songs played, years served, reason for retirement)

## The Five Silences

Five times in 300 years, the music has stopped. Each incident is remembered with horror and taught to children as a warning.

### The First Silence (Year 3 AS)
**Duration:** 47 minutes
**Cause:** The original record player's motor failed
**Panic Level:** Extreme — people didn't yet understand how quickly the virus activated
**Resolution:** Marc Delacroix jury-rigged a hand-crank solution while drummers maintained continuous rhythm
**Legacy:** The backup hand-crank system was formalized; spare parts were prioritized in expeditions

### The Second Silence (Year 67 AS)
**Duration:** 12 minutes
**Cause:** Needle #2 was dropped during maintenance
**Panic Level:** High
**Resolution:** Needle #3 was installed faster than any before or since
**Legacy:** The needle-changing ritual was formalized to prevent accidents

### The Third Silence (Year 134 AS)
**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Cause:** Flooding damaged the primary speaker wiring
**Panic Level:** Moderate (drums continued; only music stopped)
**Resolution:** The Bouches — then just a family of talented hummmers — stepped in with vocal performances
**Legacy:** The Bouches' role as emergency backup was formalized

### The Fourth Silence (Year 201 AS)
**Duration:** 3 minutes
**Cause:** A child knocked over the record player during a tantrum
**Panic Level:** Low (resolved quickly)
**Resolution:** The player was righted; the record (a Brassens) was scratched but playable
**Legacy:** Children are now forbidden from La Salle Piaf during active broadcasts

### The Fifth Silence (Year 278 AS)
**Duration:** 58 minutes
**Cause:** Unknown electrical failure knocked out all speakers simultaneously
**Panic Level:** THE GREAT PANIC
**Resolution:** The Bouches performed continuously for nearly an hour while engineers traced and repaired the fault
**Legacy:** Jérôme "Beatbox" Bouche became a community hero; the Bouches' status was elevated to "emergency essential"

## The Bouches

*See [major_families.md](major_families.md) for full family details*

The Bouches are the emergency backup. When technology fails, human voices take over.

**Origin:** The Bouche family has always been musical — but their beatboxing tradition began with Lucien Bouche (Year 89-167 AS), who could imitate drum sounds with his mouth. He taught his children. They taught theirs.

**Year 300 Status:** Every Bouche learns to beatbox. It's not optional. Family dinners involve practice sessions. Children compete to create new sounds.

**Emergency Protocol:**
When the Vinyl Guardian calls "BOUCHES!", whoever is nearest must immediately begin performing. Within minutes, multiple Bouches should be providing layered vocal percussion — enough sound to keep the virus dormant until technology is restored.

**The Great Panic (Year 278 AS):**
Jérôme Bouche was eating dinner when the speakers died. He ran to La Salle Piaf, grabbed the microphone connected to the backup speaker (still working on battery), and began beatboxing. For 58 minutes, he performed continuously — switching styles, adding vocal bass lines, occasionally singing fragments of songs. His throat was destroyed for weeks afterward. He is now considered a community hero.

**Famous Bouche Sounds:**
- The "Piaf Punch" — a beat that mimics the rhythm of *Non, Je Ne Regrette Rien*
- The "Brassens Roll" — a gentler pattern based on guitar strumming
- The "Emergency Pulse" — the standard crisis beat, designed for maximum volume and sustainability

## The Guardian's Oath

Spoken at the beginning of each term:

> "I accept The Collection. I accept The Needle. I accept The Schedule.
>
> I swear that music will not stop while I live. I swear that sound will reach every corner of our home. I swear that the songs of the Before will continue into the After.
>
> If I fail, may my song become *Petit Papa Noël*."

The final line is both a joke and a genuine threat. One Guardian who failed catastrophically — during the Third Silence — actually had their song changed by Council vote. They lived with it for 40 years.
