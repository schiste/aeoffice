## Purpose

This document catalogs the books, games, films, TV shows, and other media that inform the AD&D project's tone, themes, and references. When in doubt about how something should *feel*, consult these sources.

We're not making copies. We're making a conversation with these works — honoring them, subverting them, and occasionally stealing their best ideas while filing off the serial numbers.

## Tier 1: Core Influences (Know These Intimately)

These are the foundational texts. If you're writing for this project, you should be familiar with all of them.

### Video Games

#### **Fallout Series** (1997-present)
| Game | Year | What We're Taking |
|------|------|-------------------|
| Fallout (1997) | 1997 | Tone, dark humor, moral ambiguity, the tutorial radroach moment |
| Fallout 2 (1998) | 1998 | Factions with competing ideologies, nobody is fully "good" |
| Fallout 3 (2008) | 2008 | Environmental storytelling, the Vault-Dweller emergence narrative |
| Fallout: New Vegas (2010) | 2010 | **The gold standard.** Faction complexity, player agency, consequences |
| Fallout 4 (2015) | 2015 | Settlement building, base management, companion depth |
| Fallout 76 (2018) | 2018 | (Learn from its mistakes) |

**Specific elements to reference:**
- The radroach as a tutorial creature → Our ratroach (Dweller Bane)
- "War never changes" pessimism → "Humanity remains consistently, frustratingly human"
- The Vault-Tec experiments → The Sounding Five's "noble intentions"
- Faction moral ambiguity → Every faction is wrong in different ways
- S.P.E.C.I.A.L. stats → Our own stats system (different but inspired)
- Holotapes and terminal entries → Water-damaged documents, journal fragments
- The Pip-Boy → (We don't have an equivalent; deliberately avoiding)

**Key Fallout moments to echo:**
1. Leaving the Vault for the first time (→ leaving the caves, seeing the Crystal)
2. The first radroach fight (→ Dweller Bane encounter)
3. Discovering the world ended because of human stupidity (→ discovering the Sounding Five)
4. Faction recruitment pitches (→ our faction introductions)
5. "Was it worth it?" moral reckonings (→ the truth about the conspiracy)

#### **This War of Mine** (2014)
**What we're taking:**
- Civilian survival perspective, not military
- The emotional weight of resource decisions
- "Sadness" as a gameplay mechanic
- Scavenging at night vs. resting
- The cost of violence on non-combatants

**What we're taking:**
- City-building under existential threat
- Moral compromises for survival (child labor, extended shifts)
- Hope vs. Discontent as gameplay mechanics
- The "Book of Laws" progression system

#### **Darkest Dungeon** (2016)

### Literature

#### **A Canticle for Leibowitz** by Walter M. Miller Jr. (1959)
**What we're taking:**
- Post-apocalyptic preservation of knowledge
- The cyclical nature of human civilization
- Religious communities as keepers of the flame
- The Memorabilia → Our Archive
- Dark humor about rebuilding
- The tragedy of history repeating

**Key quote:** "You don't have a soul, Doctor. You are a soul. You have a body, temporarily."

---

#### **The Road** by Cormac McCarthy (2006)
**What we're taking:**
- The bleakness (in measured doses)
- Parent-child survival narrative
- "Carrying the fire" as hope metaphor
- Sparse, haunting prose style
- The threat of cannibalism (implied, not dwelt upon)
- The coast as destination/hope

**What we're NOT taking:**
- The complete hopelessness (we need gameplay)
- The unnamed characters (our world has names)

---

#### **Station Eleven** by Emily St. John Mandel (2014)
**What we're taking:**
- "Survival is insufficient" as a theme
- Art and culture mattering post-apocalypse
- Non-linear timeline storytelling
- Small connections between characters across time
- The Traveling Symphony → Our musicians, storytellers
- The Museum of Civilization → Our Archive

---

#### **The Book of the New Sun** by Gene Wolfe (1980-1983)
**What we're taking:**
- Unreliable narration
- Deep lore that rewards attention
- Technology so old it seems like magic
- The Torturers' Guild as inspiration for our factions
- Layered mysteries

---

#### **World War Z** by Max Brooks (2006)
**What we're taking:**
- The oral history format
- Multiple perspectives on the same apocalypse
- Institutional failure as horror
- The "Great Panic" as a model for our early days
- Survival through adaptation

## Reference Usage Guidelines

### When Writing Lore

1. **Echo, don't copy.** Dweller Bane is a *wink* at Fallout's radroach, not a replica.
2. **Subvert expectations.** If a trope is too familiar, twist it.
3. **Layer references.** Rewards for those who catch them, invisible to those who don't.
4. **Prefer obscure to obvious.** A *Canticle for Leibowitz* reference is better than a *Walking Dead* reference.

### When Designing Encounters

1. **Ask: "What would Fallout do?"** Then ask: "Is that too predictable?"
2. **Ask: "What would Metro do?"** For underground atmosphere.
3. **Ask: "What would This War of Mine do?"** For emotional weight.

### When Writing Dialogue

1. **Channel Disco Elysium** for internal monologue.
2. **Channel Fallout: New Vegas** for faction representatives.
3. **Channel The Road** for moments of despair.
4. **Channel Station Eleven** for moments of hope.

### Easter Eggs We Want

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Names** | Characters named after authors (subtle), locations named after games (even more subtle) |
| **Items** | Objects that reference source material without breaking immersion |
| **Dialogue** | Quotes adapted to fit our world |
| **Situations** | Scenarios that rhyme with famous moments from influences |

### Easter Eggs We Don't Want

- Direct quotes that break immersion ("War never changes")
- Fourth-wall breaks
- References so obscure nobody gets them
- References so obvious they're groan-worthy
- Crossover content (no actual Vault-Boy, no NCR flags)

## Anti-Influences: What We're Avoiding

### Tropes to Subvert or Skip

| Trope | Why We're Avoiding |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Zombies** | Overdone; our creatures are mutated animals, not undead |
| **Chosen One narrative** | The Hero is special (Resilient) but not prophesied |
| **Pure good vs. pure evil** | Every faction is compromised |
| **Technology = salvation** | The Engineers might be wrong |
| **Religion = ignorance** | The Choir has valid points |
| **Lone wolf survival** | Community is mechanically required |
| **Rape as characterization** | Not in our world |
| **Grimdark nihilism** | Dark, but not hopeless |
| **American-centric** | This is France, this is Europe |

### Media We're Deliberately NOT Emulating

| Source | Why |
|--------|-----|
| **The Walking Dead** (TV) | Soap opera with zombies; tone mismatch |
| **Hunger Games** | Young adult; different audience |
| **Divergent/Maze Runner** | Faction-as-identity is too neat |
| **Waterworld** | Tone too campy |
| **The Book of Eli** | Religious messaging too heavy-handed |
