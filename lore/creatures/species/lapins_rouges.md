## Overview

Before The Silence, European rabbits (*Oryctolagus cuniculus*) were prey animals. Small, skittish, dependent on hearing for predator detection.

Jonathan Reeves worried about them specifically:

> "They're the most vulnerable in my assessment. Small neural capacity, but they rely heavily on hearing for predator detection. If Hush causes even minor auditory disruption, they'd lose their primary survival mechanism. They'd be easy prey for everything."

The others reassured him. The threshold was safe. The margin was enormous.

The rabbits would be fine.

---

## What Actually Happened

Jonathan was right about one thing: Hush disrupted their hearing. Sub-lethally, as predicted, but enough to destabilize their primary survival sense.

For a few months, rabbits died in enormous numbers. Easy prey for everything.

Then evolution kicked in.

**The Selection Pressure:**

Only rabbits with compensating adaptations survived:
- Those with faster reflexes
- Those with better smell/vibration detection
- Those with more aggressive defensive behavior
- Those that bred faster

Within three years, European rabbit populations had collapsed to 5% of pre-Silence levels.

Within ten years, they had recovered to 15%.

But they were not the same rabbits.

## Les Lapins Rouges: Physical Description

**Appearance:**

A Lapin Rouge looks like a rabbit. At first glance. From a distance. In poor lighting.

| Trait | European Rabbit (Pre-Silence) | Lapin Rouge (Year 10 AS) |
|-------|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Size | 1.5-2.5 kg | 8-15 kg |
| Height | 20-30 cm | 45-60 cm (knee-height on human) |
| Ear length | 6-8 cm | 15-20 cm (hyper-developed) |
| Coloration | Brown/grey | Reddish-brown, darker markings |
| Claws | Normal | Elongated, hardened |
| Teeth | Herbivore standard | Enlarged incisors |
| Eyes | Normal | Enlarged, more forward-facing |

**The Red Coloration:**

The "rouge" in their name comes from:
1. Reddish-brown fur (mutation)
2. The blood of whatever just annoyed them

They're still technically herbivores. They just became very defensive herbivores.

## Behavioral Adaptations

### Hearing Compensation

Lapin Rouge ears are nearly twice the length of their ancestors'. They've developed:
- Extended frequency range detection
- Directional sound processing
- Vibration sensitivity through ear-to-ground contact
- Possible infrasound detection

Their hearing is now *better* than pre-Silence rabbits. Much better.

They hear you coming. They decide whether to flee or fight.

### The Claws

When hearing failed as a defense mechanism, rabbits with naturally larger claws survived predator encounters. This trait was strongly selected.

Modern Lapin Rouge claws are:
- 3-4 cm long
- Curved and sharpened through use
- **Venomous** (see below)

### The Venom

This was unexpected.

European rabbits are not venomous. Were not venomous. Somewhere in the accelerated evolution, a mutation emerged that repurposed certain glands.

Lapin Rouge claws deliver a mild venom that causes:
- Intense localized pain
- Swelling
- Temporary numbness
- Occasional systemic symptoms in sensitive individuals

Not lethal to healthy adults. Extremely unpleasant. Potentially dangerous to children or those with allergies.

**Suspected mechanism:** Modified scent gland secretion, delivered via claw grooves.

### Pack Behavior

Pre-Silence rabbits lived in warrens but were not cooperative hunters.

Lapins Rouges have developed coordinated defensive behavior. A threatened warren responds as a group:

1. **Sentinel warning** — One rabbit detects threat, alerts others
2. **Assessment** — Group determines threat level
3. **Response** — Either flee (to warren) or charge (if cornered or protecting young)

A single Lapin Rouge is a nuisance.

A warren of 20+ charging in coordinated waves is a serious problem.

## Ecology

### Diet

Still herbivorous. But aggressively herbivorous.

Lapins Rouges defend food sources with lethal determination. Gardens near their warrens are effectively unusable. They will charge humans attempting to harvest "their" plants.

### Predators

Reduced, but not eliminated. Larger predators (wolves, mutated foxes) still hunt them. But the calculus has changed:

**Pre-Silence:** Predator catches rabbit easily.
**Post-Silence:** Predator catches rabbit, rabbit retaliates, predator decides maybe not worth it.

Some predators have adapted to specialize in Lapin Rouge hunting. Others have learned to avoid them.

### Territory

Lapin Rouge warrens are larger and more aggressively defended than their ancestors'. Average territory: 2-3 km radius from central warren.

They mark boundaries with scent. They patrol. They remember intruders.

### Reproduction

If anything, faster than pre-Silence rabbits.

Gestation: 28-30 days
Litter size: 8-12 kits
Maturation: 4-5 months
Breeding age: Year-round

A single warren can expand from 20 to 200 individuals in two years under favorable conditions.

## Distribution

**Common:** Loire Valley, central France, most of former Western Europe

**Uncommon:** Northern Europe (climate limitations), urban ruins (insufficient food)

**Absent:** Underground (prefer surface warrens)

## Threat Assessment

| Scenario | Threat Level | Recommendation |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Single Lapin Rouge, unprovoked | LOW | Avoid, do not engage |
| Single Lapin Rouge, cornered | MEDIUM | Retreat, protect extremities |
| Warren, passing through territory | MEDIUM | Move quietly, do not stop |
| Warren, near young/food source | HIGH | Retreat immediately |
| Warren, aggressive posture | VERY HIGH | Defensive measures required |

### Engagement Protocols

**If attacked:**
- Protect head and throat
- Kick don't punch (reach advantage)
- Noise may disperse or attract more (situational)
- If venomed, clean wound immediately, seek medical attention

**Prevention:**
- Avoid known warren territories
- Don't establish gardens near warrens
- Don't corner individuals
- Travel in groups (less likely to be seen as threats)

## Cultural Impact

### The Irony

The Sounding Five spent three months debating animal protection. Jonathan Reeves asked about rabbits specifically. Four people told him "the rabbits will be fine."

The phrase "The rabbits will be fine" has become idiomatic in survivor communities. It means:

> "We were wrong about something in a way that seems obvious in retrospect."

**Usage:**
> "We thought the north tunnel was stable." / "The rabbits will be fine."
>
> "They said the harvest would last through winter." / "The rabbits will be fine."

### In Les Grottes de la Bresme

There is a Lapin Rouge skull mounted in The Wine Cathedral.

It was killed during the "Fluffle Incident" of Year 3 AS. Thomas — yes, *that* Thomas — killed it with a shovel while it was attacking another expedition member.

The skull is larger than most people expect.

Below it, someone has written:

> *"Les lapins vont bien."*
> *(The rabbits are fine.)*

## Research Notes

### Year 3 AS — First Classification

Initial reports of "large aggressive rabbits" dismissed as exaggeration. Subsequent evidence revised assessment.

### Year 5 AS — Venom Discovery

Expedition member scratched by Lapin Rouge developed unexpected symptoms. Analysis confirmed venomous capability.

### Year 8 AS — Behavioral Study

Long-term observation documented coordinated defense patterns. Classification changed from "individual threat" to "pack threat."

### Year 10 AS — Population Assessment

Lapin Rouge populations have stabilized at approximately 15% of pre-Silence rabbit numbers, but individual threat level significantly higher.

## The Final Question

During the Great Animal Ethics Debate, Jonathan Reeves asked:

> "If evolution takes them somewhere strange, somewhere we couldn't predict, I hope it's somewhere good."

The Lapins Rouges evolved into apex defensive herbivores. They're thriving. They've claimed their territory. They defend it with surprising effectiveness.

Is that "somewhere good"?

For the rabbits, probably yes.

For everyone else...

The rabbits are fine.
