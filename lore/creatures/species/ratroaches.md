## Overview

Nobody asked for this. Nobody wanted this. And yet, here we are.

The Ratroach is what happens when Hush destabilizes the genetic barriers that keep species separate, and two of humanity's oldest pest companions decide to become something worse together.

It shouldn't be possible. A mammal-insect hybrid violates everything we thought we knew about biology. But Hush wasn't interested in what should be possible. It was interested in what could survive.

The rats could survive. The roaches could survive. Together, they could survive *better*.

---

## The Hybrid Question

**"How is this even biologically possible?"**

It isn't. Or it wasn't. Pre-Silence biology would have called this a fever dream. But Hush didn't just kill humans — it destabilized the genetic expression of everything it touched. In animals, this created accelerated mutation pressure. In some cases, it broke down the barriers between species entirely.

The first ratroaches appeared in environments where rats and cockroaches shared close quarters: subway systems, sewer networks, building basements. The current theory suggests:

1. Hush-damaged rats and roaches co-inhabited the same spaces
2. A virus (possibly a mutated strain of Hush itself) facilitated horizontal gene transfer
3. Offspring inherited traits from both lineages
4. Selection pressure favored the hybrids

The result is a creature that is, genetically speaking, about 70% rat and 30% cockroach. The percentages vary by individual and regional population. Some look almost entirely like rats with a few chitinous patches. Others are... harder to look at.

**The Uncomfortable Truth:**
> "We're not sure if they're one species that looks different depending on environment, or several species that share enough DNA to interbreed. We're also not sure we want to know."
> — Research notes, Year 45 AS

## Biological Profile

### Physical Characteristics

| Attribute | Brown Rat (Pre-Silence) | German Cockroach (Pre-Silence) | Ratroach (Year 10 AS) |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Length (body)** | 20-25 cm | 1.3-1.6 cm | 30-40 cm |
| **Weight** | 0.3-0.5 kg | ~0.001 kg | 0.8-1.5 kg |
| **Coloration** | Brown/grey | Tan/brown | Dark brown, chitinous patches |
| **Distinguishing Features** | Standard rat | Standard roach | Segmented back plates, vestigial antennae, hybrid morphology |

### The Look

A ratroach looks like a rat. From a distance. In poor lighting. If you're not paying attention.

Up close, the differences become apparent — and unsettling. The face is mostly mammalian: a rat's snout, whiskers, small dark eyes. But the back tells another story. Chitinous plates run along the spine, segmented like armor. The hindquarters have that articulated, insectoid quality. And where ears should be, there are vestigial antennae — non-functional in most individuals, but present, twitching occasionally, reminding you that this creature shouldn't exist.

The fur and chitin alternate in patterns that vary by individual. Some are mostly furry with chitin only on the back. Others have patches distributed seemingly at random across their bodies. The overall effect is deeply wrong — your brain recognizes "rat" and "roach" separately but cannot process them together.

**First Reaction (universal):**
> "Is that a—"
> *pause*
> "What is that?"

### Morphology Details

**Body Type:** Quadruped (rodent-insectoid hybrid)

**Integument (Skin/Covering):**
- Type: Fur and chitin (hybrid)
- Texture: Fur is coarse; chitin is smooth and segmented
- Special Properties: Chitinous plates provide natural armor on back and flanks; resistant to crushing; slightly resistant to piercing; unfortunately makes them crunchy if stepped on

**Thermoregulation:**
- [x] Warm-blooded (endothermic)
- Note: The chitinous sections have reduced blood flow but the creature maintains mammalian body temperature

**Limbs & Locomotion:**
- Primary movement: Running (quadruped)
- Secondary: Climbing, squeezing through gaps
- Speed: Fast (3) — matches a running human; hope you stretched
- Special: Can flatten body to squeeze through gaps 40% smaller than body width; climbs vertical surfaces if textured; excellent jumpers (up to 1m horizontal)

**Dentition & Claws:**
- Teeth: Rodent incisors (continuously growing); can gnaw through wood, soft metal, plastic, bone, your optimism
- Claws: Sharp, curved; functional for climbing and digging
- Weapons: Primary weapon is bite; claws are secondary; neither is venomous (small mercies)

## Sensory Systems

### The Sound Question

Ratroaches occupy an interesting position in the post-Silence sound ecology. They retained functional hearing — enhanced, in fact — but their relationship to sound is primarily defensive.

**Hearing:**

| Attribute | Rating | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Range (Hz) | 200-90,000 Hz | Extended ultrasonic range from both ancestral species |
| Sensitivity | Enhanced | Detects sounds inaudible to humans |
| Directional | Excellent | Antennae provide additional directional input |
| Vibration Detection | Advanced | Through ground and air; compensation from roach ancestry |

**Sound Behavior Classification:**
- [x] **Silence Hunter** — Hunts by detecting sound; silence = safety from it
- Note: Ratroaches prefer to avoid confrontation and use sound primarily for threat detection. They're not aggressive hunters — just very persistent scavengers who know when you're coming.

**Reaction to Common Sounds:**

| Sound Type | Reaction |
|------------|----------|
| Human speech | Flees (unless cornered or defending territory) |
| Drums/rhythm | Flees (associates with human presence) |
| Crystal resonance | Agitated; avoids Crystal sites |
| Silence (prolonged) | More active; emerges from hiding |
| Sudden loud noise | Freezes momentarily, then scatters |

**The Irony:**
They hear better than pre-Silence rats. They just use that hearing to avoid you more effectively. Progress?

### Other Senses

**Vision:**
- Quality: Moderate (poor in bright light, good in darkness)
- Adaptations: Excellent night vision; motion-based detection
- Eye placement: Side-facing (wide field of view)

**Smell:**
- Quality: Excellent
- Can detect: Food, predators, other ratroaches, humans, water sources, fear (allegedly)

**Other Sensory Adaptations:**
- Whisker mapping (spatial awareness in darkness)
- Antennae sensitivity to air currents (roach inheritance)
- Can detect slight air pressure changes (knows when doors open)

## Ecology & Behavior

### Diet & Feeding

**Classification:**
- [x] Omnivore
- [x] Scavenger

**Primary Food Sources:**
1. Organic detritus (they're not picky)
2. Stored human food (they know where you keep it)
3. Other invertebrates
4. Plant matter
5. Each other (in extreme circumstances)
6. Literally anything organic (this is not an exaggeration)

**Hunting/Foraging Method:**
Ratroaches don't hunt in the traditional sense. They scavenge, opportunistically and efficiently. A single ratroach can consume 10% of its body weight daily. A nest of twenty can strip a food storage area overnight.

They're also patient. They've learned that humans eventually leave food unattended. They wait.

**Will Eat Humans?**
- [ ] Opportunistically — if easy/dying
- Note: Ratroaches will consume human corpses (they're scavengers) but do not attack living humans for food. They attack for defense or territory. The eating comes after, if you're unlucky enough to die in their territory.

### Habitat & Distribution

**Preferred Environment:**
- [x] Underground/Caves
- [x] Urban ruins
- [x] Abandoned structures
- Note: Anywhere with darkness, warmth, and organic matter. They've inherited the adaptability of both parent species.

**Geographic Range:**
- Common: Urban ruins throughout former Europe; anywhere humans lived densely
- Uncommon: Rural areas (populations exist but are sparser)
- Absent: High altitude (above 2000m), extreme cold, deep desert

**Territory Size:** Variable; 50-200m radius from nest depending on food availability

**Shelter:**
- Type: Nest (communal)
- Location preference: Enclosed spaces with multiple exits; walls, under floors, inside machinery, abandoned furniture
- Quality: Elaborate tunnel systems; temperature-regulated; multiple chambers for different purposes

### Reproduction

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Breeding Season** | Year-round (no rest for the weary) |
| **Mating System** | Polygamous |
| **Gestation** | 21-24 days |
| **Offspring per Cycle** | 6-12 pups |
| **Parental Care** | Mother only; pups independent at 4 weeks |
| **Maturation Time** | 8-10 weeks |
| **Lifespan** | 3-8 years (wild); up to 8 years in stable conditions |

**The Math:**
A single breeding pair can produce 60+ offspring per year. Those offspring reach breeding age in 10 weeks. Do not let them establish a nest.

### Social Structure

- [x] Colonial

**Group Terminology:**
- A group is called a: "Mischief" (traditional rat term, now used ironically) or "Horror" (more accurate)

**Hierarchy:**
Ratroach colonies have loose hierarchies based on size and age. Larger, older individuals get first access to food and preferred nesting spots. There's no clear "alpha" — more of a general "bigger ones eat first" understanding.

They recognize each other individually and maintain complex social relationships. They also hold grudges.

| Scenario | Threat Level | Recommended Action |

| Single individual, fleeing | None | Let it flee; it wants this even less than you do |

### Combat/Defense Information

**Natural Weapons:**
- Teeth: Sharp incisors; can penetrate leather; infection risk is primary concern
- Claws: Secondary; used for gripping during bite
- Chitin: Not a weapon but provides defense; difficult to kill via crushing

**Defensive Capabilities:**
- Numbers (they're never alone)
- Speed and agility
- Can squeeze through gaps you didn't know existed
- Chitinous armor absorbs some impact
- Excellent at hiding

**Weaknesses:**
- Individual specimens are fragile (chitin protects back, not everything)
- Sensitive to bright light
- Fire is effective (if you don't mind the smell)
- Cannot survive long cold exposure
- Predictable patrol patterns if observed

**Engagement Protocols:**
1. Do not corner them (they will fight)
2. Seal entry points to prevent nest establishment
3. Remove food sources systematically
4. If removal required: traps, fire, or flooding (caves)
5. Never reach into a space you cannot see

**Deterrents:**
- Strong smells (mint, ammonia)
- Fire/smoke
- Bright light
- Ultrasonic deterrents (mixed results)
- Cats (surprisingly effective; cats don't care that they're weird)

## Origin & Evolution

### Pre-Silence Ancestors

**Original Species:**
- Brown Rat (*Rattus norvegicus*) — The common rat; humanity's shadow for 10,000 years
- German Cockroach (*Blattella germanica*) — The smaller, indoor-dwelling roach

**Original Characteristics:**
Both species were synanthropic — adapted to live alongside humans. Both were considered pests. Both were survivors.

Rats: Intelligent, social, adaptable, excellent breeders.
Roaches: Ancient, resilient, could survive nearly anything, excellent breeders.

**Role in Ecosystem:**
Both served as decomposers and food sources for larger predators. Both thrived wherever humans created waste and shelter.

### The Mutation

**Initial Impact of Hush:**

Hush's effect on rats and roaches was similar to other animals: sub-lethal disruption of neural pathways, particularly auditory processing. For most species, this was the end of the story — they survived with minor sensory changes.

But in the confined spaces of the Paris Metro (Year 3-8 AS), something different happened.

The current hypothesis:

1. **Extreme proximity** — Thousands of rats and millions of roaches trapped together in metro tunnels when the surface became deadly silent
2. **Hush mutation** — A variant of Hush (or a secondary virus it enabled) acted as a horizontal gene transfer vector
3. **Chimera generation** — Offspring began expressing traits from both lineages
4. **Selection pressure** — The hybrids proved more resilient than either parent species alone

**Selection Pressures:**
1. **Predation** — Mutated predators couldn't tell what to make of them; confusion = survival
2. **Environment** — Chitin provided protection in rough terrain; fur maintained warmth
3. **Food competition** — Larger size allowed access to resources smaller roaches couldn't reach
4. **Disease resistance** — Hybrid vigor provided immunity advantages

**Key Adaptations (in order of emergence):**
1. Year 3-5 AS: First chimeric offspring observed (likely dismissed as deformed rats)
2. Year 6-7 AS: Stable breeding population established in Paris Metro
3. Year 8 AS: First documented encounter; officially classified as new species
4. Year 10-15 AS: Spread to sewer systems across former France
5. Year 25 AS: Found in most urban ruins in former Western Europe
6. Year 50 AS: Population stabilized; considered endemic pest

**Ongoing Evolution:**
Ratroaches appear to have stabilized as a species. Regional variants exist (Metro ratroaches are darker; rural specimens are smaller) but they're all clearly the same species. They breed true.

They're not going away.

## Cultural Significance

### In Survivor Communities

**Common Names/Slang:**
- "Ratroach" — The standard term; efficient, descriptive
- "Rat-cafard" — French term (rat-cockroach)
- "Chitinback" — Reference to the armored spine
- "That thing" — Universal
- "Oh no" — Also universal; reaction upon seeing one

**Idioms/Sayings:**
> "Rats will be rats" — Meaning: Some problems are eternal; acceptance of the unchangeable

> "Built like a ratroach" — Meaning: Surprisingly resilient; hard to kill; possibly an insult

> "Found a chitinback in the pantry" — Meaning: Discovered a problem that's actually ten problems

**Folklore/Myths:**
In some settlements, ratroaches are considered bad luck — a sign that a location is "too dead to live in." The logic: if ratroaches have fully colonized a space, the original inhabitants failed.

Others view them with a grudging respect. They survived The Silence. They adapted. They persisted. In their own horrible way, they're success stories.

**The Paris Metro Legend:**
Old survivors claim the original ratroaches weren't accidents — that someone in the Metro tried to "improve" rats with roach DNA to create better scavengers. This is almost certainly nonsense. But it's the kind of story people tell about creatures that shouldn't exist.

### Utility (If Any)

- [ ] **Edible** — Technically yes; tastes like regret; not recommended unless starving; prepare thoroughly to avoid parasites
- [x] **Materials** — Chitin plates can be harvested for small armor pieces or tool reinforcement; not worth the effort for most
- [ ] **Domesticable** — No. Absolutely not. Why would you want this?
- [ ] **Trainable** — Rats can be trained; ratroaches theoretically could be but nobody has tried seriously and please don't

### The Irony

Marie Fontaine worried about rats during the Great Animal Ethics Debate. She wasn't concerned about protecting them — she was concerned about what they might become.

From the debate transcript:
> "Rats are survivors. If we destabilize their neural pathways even slightly, we're not going to kill them. We're going to make them... different. And rats that are 'different' in an apocalypse? That's not a comforting thought."

She was specifically worried about rats evolving to better exploit the post-Silence world.

She did not anticipate that "evolving" would include "borrowing DNA from cockroaches."

But her point stands: rats are survivors. Now they're survivors with chitin armor and the reproductive rate of both parent species.

The rats adapted. They always do.

## Research History

### Discovery & Classification

**First Sighting:** Year 5 AS, Paris Metro (unofficial; dismissed as "weird-looking rat")

**First Documentation:** Year 8 AS, Paris Metro, by Mathieu Chen (survivor, former veterinarian)

**Initial Assessment:** "Either I'm hallucinating, or this rat has segments on its back. I'm going to assume I'm hallucinating because the alternative is worse."

**Classification History:**
- Year 8: First specimens captured; classified as "deformed *Rattus norvegicus*"
- Year 12: Breeding populations confirmed; reclassified as possible new subspecies
- Year 18: Genetic analysis (crude, pre-Silence equipment) confirmed hybrid traits; new species designation proposed
- Year 25: *Rattus blattodea mutatis* officially adopted as scientific name
- Year 50: Accepted as endemic species throughout former Western Europe

### Notable Incidents

**The Metro Emergence (Year 8 AS):**
The first confirmed ratroach encounter. Mathieu Chen, exploring the Paris Metro for supplies, found what he initially thought was a deformed rat. When it turned and he saw the chitinous back plates and vestigial antennae, he reportedly said: "I'm going back to the surface. The monsters there make sense."

**The Ardèche Infiltration (Year 34 AS):**
A ratroach population established itself in the ventilation systems of an Ardèche settlement. The infestation was discovered when a child reported "the walls are crunchy." Removal took three months and required partial flooding of the tunnel system.

**The Great Ratroach Panic (Year 67 AS):**
A rumor spread that ratroaches could crossbreed with normal rats, creating "super-ratroaches." This was false — ratroaches breed true and cannot hybridize further — but it caused several settlements to exterminate their entire rat populations "just in case." The food storage losses from the resulting insect explosions were worse than the ratroaches ever were.

**Dweller Bane (Year 300 AS):**
The most famous individual ratroach. See [Dweller Bane](../unique/dweller_bane.md) for full documentation.

### Current Research

**Open Questions:**
1. How did the original hybridization occur? (Horizontal gene transfer theory is best guess, but mechanism unclear)
2. Are ratroaches still capable of further hybridization with other species?
3. Why do some populations show more chitinous coverage than others?
4. What's the upper limit of their lifespan? (Dweller Bane suggests it may be much longer than assumed)

**Active Studies:**
Most settlements are more interested in extermination than research. However, the Archive is reportedly collecting specimens for long-term study. Their findings are, as always, not shared.

## Quick Identification Guide

### "Is That a Ratroach?" Checklist

- [ ] Size: Larger than typical rat (30-40 cm body length)
- [ ] Body shape: Rat-like but with visible segmentation on back
- [ ] Coloration: Dark brown; may have visible chitin patches
- [ ] Movement: Runs like a rat but "clicks" on hard surfaces (chitin tapping)
- [ ] Head: Rat face with vestigial antennae where ears should be
- [ ] Sound: Squeaks like a rat; also makes a soft chittering (chitin plates rubbing)
- [ ] Reaction: Flees from humans; much faster than expected

### Similar Species (Avoid Confusion)

| Species | Key Difference |
|---------|----------------|
| Brown rat | No chitin; smaller; normal ears; no antennae |
| Large cockroach | No fur; completely insectoid; much smaller |
| Mole rat | Underground only; no chitin; different body shape |
| Shrew | Much smaller; pointed snout; no chitin |

### Warning Signs of Presence

- Droppings (rat-like but slightly larger; may contain chitin fragments)
- Gnaw marks on unusual materials (they can chew through more than regular rats)
- Chitinous fragments near entry points (they molt occasionally)
- "Clicking" sounds in walls at night
- The smell (similar to rats but with an additional musty, insectoid undertone)
- Your food is disappearing faster than it should
